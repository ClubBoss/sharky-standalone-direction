# Transfer Coverage Packet v1

Status: ACTIVE
Last updated: 2026-05-06

## Purpose

Define how the course creates real-play transfer and return motivation across
the route.

This packet exists because a great learning app does not only teach concepts
inside the app.
It makes the learner start seeing poker differently away from the app too.

That is one of the strongest long-term habit drivers:

1. the learner notices real improvement
2. the app becomes useful between sessions, not only during them
3. return feels valuable, not forced
4. subscription later feels like expansion of a trusted habit

## Core Rule

Every release-visible playable world should create one small real-play behavior.

Not a vague promise.
Not a future advanced skill.
One concrete thing the learner can notice, name, or do in a real hand, stream,
or observation spot.

## Transfer Design Standard

A good transfer seam is:

1. short
2. observation-friendly
3. tied to the world's main cognitive shift
4. achievable without advanced live pressure
5. strong enough to create an "I noticed this for real" moment

Transfer should make the learner feel:

- "I used this outside the app"
- "I can see the game more clearly now"
- "I want to come back because the next world will upgrade how I play again"

## Anti-Pattern Rule

Do not use transfer seams that:

1. require strong real-money courage too early
2. demand advanced multi-street execution before the concept is stable
3. feel like homework punishment
4. depend on external tooling
5. pretend a beginner has already mastered the world

## Retention Relationship

Transfer is not only a learning device.
It is one of the cleanest return loops in the product.

Why:

1. the learner gets proof that the app matters in real poker
2. the learner starts carrying the app's concepts into daily play or viewing
3. that creates earned anticipation for the next session
4. the habit grows from usefulness, not guilt or pressure

This is the preferred route to future subscription durability:

value -> visible improvement -> return habit -> deeper paid value later

## Transfer Types

Use only lightweight transfer types in the shared early core:

### Type A. Observation transfer

The learner watches for a pattern in real play, video, or a stream.

Examples:

- spot BTN before the cards are dealt
- say who acts first on the flop
- identify whether a bet wants calls or folds

### Type B. Naming transfer

The learner labels a visible pattern quickly in their head.

Examples:

- dry or wet board
- weak ace or dominated hand
- first-in open vs facing-open spot

### Type C. Low-risk behavior transfer

The learner applies one bounded behavior without needing deep courage or
advanced postflop skill.

Examples:

- fold more junk hands preflop
- notice when being out of position makes a hand feel worse
- avoid betting just because a hand "looks strong"

Do not require high-risk behavior transfer early.

## Transfer Density Rule

Release-visible worlds do not need many transfer tasks.

They do need:

1. at least one named transfer seam
2. a transfer task that matches the world's actual shift
3. wording that feels quick and doable

One good transfer seam is better than five generic homework prompts.

## Wording Rules

Good transfer wording:

- "Watch three hands and point to BTN before the cards are dealt."
- "Pause the flop. Say dry or wet before anyone bets."
- "Spot one bet that wants a call and one bet that wants folds."

Bad transfer wording:

- "Apply this concept in your poker life."
- "Go play and try to remember everything."
- "Master this before tomorrow."

## Relationship To Return Habit

Transfer should feed the daily-return loop.

Best pattern:

1. learn a compact concept
2. notice it in real poker
3. come back because the app now feels more useful
4. get the next concept that improves what the learner sees tomorrow

This is stronger than:

1. raw streak pressure
2. empty reward loops
3. guilt-heavy reminders

## Relationship To Monetization

Transfer is one of the strongest proofs that the product is worth paying for
later.

If the learner repeatedly feels:

- "I noticed this in a real hand"
- "I made one better choice because of the app"
- "I understand streams and tables more now"

then deeper repair, mastery, and advanced worlds become naturally monetizable
later without aggressive pressure.

## Operational Rule

Before calling a release-visible world complete, ask:

1. what real-play behavior does this world create?
2. can the learner do it away from the app within 30 to 90 seconds?
3. does it feel like a win instead of homework?
4. does it increase the chance the learner wants to come back tomorrow?

If those answers are weak, the world is still missing an important retention
layer even if the concept teaching itself is correct.
