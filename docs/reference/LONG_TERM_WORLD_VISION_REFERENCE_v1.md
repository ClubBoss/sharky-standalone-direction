## Long-Term World Vision (W13–W36)

This section is a planning anchor, not a current build commitment.

The launch-critical first volume is W1–W12. This
vision locks the intended direction so the curriculum does not drift or lose
coherence as the product grows. Do not start authoring W13+ until W12 is
release-grade and the current first-wave route is honestly stable.

### Specialization Fork

W1–W12 are the shared foundation route. Every cognitive shift in that block applies equally
to cash, MTT, 6-max, and full-ring. Do not add format-specific pressure before
the learner has range, stack, and board concepts stable.

After Volume I, the product may begin a specialization gateway and later route
learners into Cash or MTT emphasis. Full specialization should become explicit
only after the shared route has proven deeper continuity.

From the later advanced route, the ladder may fork into two optional specialization tracks:

- **Cash track**: deep stacks (100bb+), winrate per hour, table selection, no
  ICM pressure, postflop freedom.
- **MTT track**: ICM mathematics, bubble play, M-ratio stack-off ranges, final
  table dynamics, push/fold.

Table format (6-max vs full ring) should appear as a dedicated concept in
W8–W9, not earlier. Core insight: "Same hand, different format — UTG range
tightens at full ring, widens at 6-max."

Specialization tracks are also a natural premium/expansion monetization
surface: Volume I is the launch-critical core product; later Cash Path and MTT
Path are natural advanced expansions or higher-tier depth.

### Block 1 — Foundation (W1–W6)
#### Ceiling: aware novice

**W1: Poker From Zero**

Cognitive shift: the table is not chaos — it is a structured game with fixed rules,
fixed roles, and a predictable action sequence. The learner who understands the
rules completely has a foundation every future concept builds on.

Core concepts:
- Table structure: seats, dealer button, positions relative to the button
- Blinds: small blind and big blind — why they exist, who posts them
- Card ranks and suits: 2–A, four suits, no suit hierarchy at showdown
- The four streets: preflop, flop (3 cards), turn (1 card), river (1 card)
- Action sequence: preflop starts left of BB; postflop starts left of dealer
- The five actions: fold, check, bet, call, raise — when each is available
- Hand rankings: high card through royal flush — every combination in order
- The pot: what goes in, how it grows, who wins it
- Showdown: who shows, who wins, split pots
- Muck: when and why a player does not have to show
- Ante: when present, everyone pays before cards are dealt
- First repair concept: everyone folds except you — you win the pot without
  showing your cards; the best hand is not required to win

Typical drills:
- "Player A posts SB, Player B posts BB. Who acts first preflop? Who acts first
  postflop if there are three players to the flop?"
- "Board: Ah-Kh-Qh-Jh-Th. You hold 9h-2c. Does your 9h complete a royal flush
  or a straight flush? Who wins against As-2d?"
- "Name the five actions and when each is available."

What the learner can do after W1:
- Sit at any Texas Hold'em table and understand every rule-level event
- Correctly identify who acts when, and what each action costs
- Read a hand history and follow every decision without confusion

**W2: Hand Discipline**

Cognitive shift: not every starting hand has positive expected value.
The first real decision in poker is not how to play a hand — it is whether
to play it at all. Fold discipline is the first line of profit.

Core concepts:
- Starting hand value: not all 169 starting hands are equal
- Premium hands: AA, KK, QQ, JJ, AK — always worth entering the pot from any position
- Playable hands: strong broadways, suited connectors, pocket pairs below JJ —
  context-dependent; position matters
- Trash hands: most off-suit disconnected low hands lose money at showdown
  more often than they win, especially out of position
- Weak ace problem: A2o–A9o appears strong because of the Ace, but loses to
  any better ace; dominated hand: a hand that shares a strong card with the
  opponent's hand but is beaten on the second card (e.g., AT vs AK: same ace,
  worst kicker — called dominated)
- Dominated hand concept: playing dominated hands is how beginners lose their
  stacks without a bad beat; they have ~25% equity at best and are in nearly
  all unfavorable scenarios
- Kicker awareness: with two players both holding a pair of aces, the second
  card determines the winner — kicker literacy prevents losing to hands you
  "should have beaten"
- Fold as a tool, not a failure: folding a bad hand before the flop is free;
  folding after calling preflop costs money; the earlier you fold correctly,
  the cheaper the mistake

Typical drills:
- "You are UTG with Q4o. What is the correct action? Why is this different from
  Q4o on the BTN?"
- "You hold A7o. Villain holds AK. The flop comes A-8-3. Who is winning and
  by how much? Why is this hand trouble?"
- "Name two hands that look strong but are dominated traps."

What the learner can do after W2:
- Immediately recognize trash hands and fold them without second-guessing
- Identify dominated hands and avoid costly pre-commitment
- Use fold as a deliberate tool, not a sign of weakness

**W3: Position Thinking**

Cognitive shift: the same hand has different value depending on where you sit
relative to the dealer. Position is a permanent advantage that does not change
with luck or skill — it is structural.

Core concepts:
- Position names: UTG (under the gun), HJ (hijack), CO (cutoff), BTN (button),
  SB (small blind), BB (big blind)
- In position (IP): acting after the opponent on every postflop street —
  you see what they do before you decide; maximum information before each decision
- Out of position (OOP): acting before the opponent postflop — you commit chips
  without knowing what they will do next; disadvantage that cannot be fully overcome
- The BTN advantage: the button always acts last postflop; any two cards that
  could be marginal OOP become playable IP because of positional control
- Same hand, different seats: example — 7-8 suited is a strong speculative
  hand on the BTN, a marginal hand UTG; position changes playability
- Relative position: in multiway pots, your position relative to the preflop
  aggressor (the CBet-er) is as important as your absolute table seat; sitting
  directly to the left of the aggressor means you face the CBet last and have
  all other players' actions as information before you act — this is the most
  advantageous relative position; sitting directly to the right of the aggressor
  means you act first postflop with the least information; relative position
  determines how much of your absolute positional advantage you can realize
  in multiway hands and partially explains why calling from the CO when the
  BTN has also called is worse than the raw position numbers suggest
- Positional awareness in multi-way pots: when there are callers between you
  and the original raiser, acting last gives even more information
- Late position opening: CO and BTN can open wider ranges because any post-flop
  decision is made with positional advantage
- Early position tightness: UTG ranges are tighter not because the hands are
  weaker in a vacuum but because all postflop streets will be played OOP vs
  anyone who calls from later seats

Typical drills:
- "You hold 9-T suited. Is this hand playable from UTG? From the BTN? What changes?"
- "You are the BTN. Three players limped. You raise. Everyone folds except the BB.
  Who is IP? Who acts first on the flop?"
- "Name two advantages of acting last on the river."

What the learner can do after W3:
- Consistently identify who has position on who in any hand
- Adjust which hands they play based on position, not habit
- Understand why the BTN is the most profitable seat at any table

**W4: Preflop Framework**

Cognitive shift: preflop decisions are not random — they follow a logical
framework of open, call, or fold based on position, stack depth, and opponent
action. The learner who has a framework makes consistent decisions instead of
improvising every hand.

Core concepts:
- First-in open: when everyone folds to you, you have two options — raise or fold;
  limping (just calling the big blind) is a passive option that gives the pot away
  and loses positional initiative; the raise size sends a clear price signal
- Facing an open: when someone has already raised, your options are call, 3-bet,
  or fold; the decision is based on hand strength, position, and opponent range
- Open/call/fold logic: strong hands open or re-raise; speculative hands call in
  position for implied odds; weak hands fold
- Position-dependent opening ranges: BTN and CO open more hands than UTG and HJ
  because of positional advantage postflop; full ring UTG opens tighter than
  6-max UTG for the same reason
- Playing vs limpers: when one or more players just called the big blind, raising
  to isolate is almost always correct with any hand strong enough to play;
  limping behind encourages multiway pots where hand value decreases; the raise
  should be larger (add 1bb per limper beyond the standard 3x) to charge
  drawing hands and clear the field
- Blind vs blind dynamics: when everyone folds to the small blind, both players
  are in a special situation — SB is OOP but acts last preflop; BB acts last
  preflop in all other spots but first postflop; both players' ranges widen
  dramatically compared to any earlier-position spot; BB must defend wide to
  prevent the SB from stealing with near-100% frequency
- 3-bet fundamentals: re-raising the opener is a 3-bet; premium hands (AA, KK,
  QQ, AK) are clear 3-bets; certain hands (A5s, K9s) can be light 3-bet bluffs
  because they block strong calling and re-raising ranges
- Calling out of the big blind: BB gets a discount to call any raise because of
  the money already posted; this means BB can call with a wider range than any
  other position, but not unlimited — pot odds and playability still matter

Typical drills:
- "UTG opens 3x. Three players fold. You are on the BTN with KJo. Call, 3-bet,
  or fold? What changes if you are in the BB?"
- "Two players limp before the action reaches you on the CO. You hold A9o. What
  do you do? What size?"
- "SB folds to you in the BB heads up. How wide should your defense range be?
  Why is limping from SB sometimes correct here?"

What the learner can do after W4:
- Make a consistent preflop decision in under 10 seconds for any standard spot
- Adjust ranges based on position without using a chart
- Recognize when to raise vs limpers and why passive completion is a leak

**W5: Bet Purpose And Price**

Cognitive shift: a bet is not just "I have a good hand" — it is a message with
a specific purpose. The learner who understands why bets happen can read any
bet they face and make better decisions about whether to call.

Core concepts:
- Why bets happen: value (wanting to be called by worse), bluff (wanting folds),
  protection (denying free cards to draws), semi-bluff (equity + fold equity)
- Bet sizing as message: a larger bet says "I have a very strong or very weak
  hand"; a smaller bet says "I have a medium range and want many calls"
- Pot odds: the ratio of the call amount to the total pot after calling;
  if the pot is 100 and the call is 50, you are getting 3:1 (33% needed to call)
- Converting pot odds to equity requirement: if you need 33% equity to break even,
  any hand with more than 33% equity against the opponent's betting range is a call
- Bluff break-even frequency: from the bluffer's perspective, a bluff is profitable
  when the opponent folds more often than the bluff costs as a fraction of the total
  risk; formula: required fold equity = bet ÷ (pot + bet); a half-pot bluff needs
  the opponent to fold 33% of the time to break even; a pot-size bluff needs 50%;
  understanding break-even fold frequency answers the question "can I bluff here?"
  before asking "which hand do I bluff with?"; this is the bluffer's equivalent
  of pot odds — two sides of the same mathematical relationship
- Implied odds: the additional chips you can win on future streets if you hit
  your hand; a draw that does not have direct pot odds may still be profitable
  if hitting means winning a large pot
- Reverse implied odds: some hands have negative implied odds — hitting the draw
  creates the second-best hand (e.g., hitting a flush when a higher flush is
  possible); these situations make calling worse than raw equity suggests
- The variance seed: a call can be mathematically correct and still lose;
  a correct decision with a bad outcome is not a mistake — it is variance;
  the learner who confuses outcomes with decision quality will break their own
  strategy by "fixing" things that were never broken

Typical drills:
- "Pot is 80. Villain bets 40. What are your pot odds? If you have 35% equity,
  do you call or fold? Show the math."
- "You have a flush draw on the flop. The pot is 100, villain bets 100. You
  need about 34% equity to call. A flush draw has ~36% equity. Is this a call
  or a fold, and why does the answer depend on implied odds?"
- "You called correctly with a draw. The river bricks. Villain shows top pair.
  Was your call a mistake? Why not?"

What the learner can do after W5:
- Calculate pot odds on any bet in real time
- Distinguish value bet from bluff from protection from semi-bluff
- Accept a correct call that lost without changing the underlying strategy

**W6: Board And Draws**

Cognitive shift: the board is not just cards — it is a map of who has what.
Dry boards favour the preflop aggressor; wet boards give draws value.
The learner who reads the board reads the situation.

Core concepts:
- Dry board: few draws possible (e.g., K-7-2 rainbow); the board connects
  poorly with calling ranges; the preflop raiser usually has range advantage
- Wet board: many draws available (e.g., 9-8-7 two-tone); both players can
  have strong made hands or strong draws; aggression has less fold equity
- Static board vs dynamic board: a static board (A-K-2 rainbow) is one where
  few turn cards significantly change the relative hand strengths — the player
  who is ahead on the flop is very likely to remain ahead; a dynamic board
  (9-8-7 two-tone) can be dramatically altered by virtually any turn card;
  static boards support lower CBet frequencies and smaller sizes because
  the range leader is in no hurry — the board won't hurt them; dynamic boards
  demand more immediate betting to deny equity before the turn card shifts
  the balance; the static/dynamic distinction is more practically useful for
  CBet frequency decisions than dry/wet alone because it captures the
  time-pressure element: how urgent is it to charge draws right now?
- Connected board: sequential cards (T-J-Q, 7-8-9) make straights possible
- Two-tone board: two cards of the same suit make a flush draw possible
- Outs: cards that improve your hand to the likely best hand; a flush draw
  has 9 outs; an open-ended straight draw has 8 outs
- Rule of 4 and 2: multiply outs × 4 for equity when two cards remain;
  multiply outs × 2 when one card remains; approximate but accurate enough
  for real-time decisions
- Backdoor draws: a backdoor flush or straight draw needs two running cards;
  adds approximately 4% equity per backdoor draw; small but meaningful in
  close decisions
- Reverse implied odds: hitting a draw can make the second-best hand;
  if the board has possible higher flushes or straights that opponent can hold,
  the implied value of completing your draw decreases significantly; calling
  with dominated draws is a common and costly leak
- Semi-bluff: betting with a draw is profitable even when called because
  you win when: (a) opponent folds, or (b) you hit; equity + fold equity
  together create EV even without showdown value; this is the foundational
  concept for all aggressive draw play
- Double gutter (double belly buster): a straight draw that looks like two
  separate gutshots but totals 8 outs — identical to an open-ended straight
  draw; example: holding 7-9 on a T-8-5 board — a 6 or a J completes the
  straight; the board looks disconnected but the draw is strong; learners
  who identify this as a gutshot (4 outs) are severely underestimating their
  equity and making systematically wrong decisions
- Clean outs vs dirty outs: a clean out improves your hand to the best hand
  with no risk of also completing a better hand for the opponent; a dirty
  out technically completes your draw but may give the opponent a superior
  hand (e.g., a 9 that completes your straight also puts a flush on board
  if the 9 is the same suit as two existing board cards); counting only clean
  outs when facing reverse-implied-odds situations prevents overpaying for
  draws; a flush draw on a board where the opponent likely holds a higher
  flush draw has fewer than 9 clean outs
- Blocked outs: an out is blocked when the card you need is already accounted
  for — either face-up on the board or provably in the opponent's hand;
  holding a hand with a pair of tens means two tens are unavailable as set
  outs; in a multiway pot, each additional opponent in the hand reduces the
  probability that any given out is live; blocked outs reduce your equity
  below what a naive out-count suggests
- Live outs vs dead outs: a live out is a card that could still appear on a future
  street because it has not been seen in any hole cards or exposed cards;
  a dead out is a card eliminated from the deck — exposed in a folded hand
  (visible in a live game after misdeal or all-in), or known to be in
  another player's hand; in multiway pots where several hands were folded,
  any specific out card has a small but real probability of being dead;
  disciplined players adjust their equity estimate downward in large multiway
  pots to reflect this; the practical rule: in a 6-way pot a flush draw has
  closer to 7–8 live outs on average than the full 9
- Hidden outs: outs that exist but are not immediately obvious when reading the
  hand; common examples: holding a pocket pair that can improve to trips using a
  board card (the two remaining cards of your pair are hidden outs to set or
  trips); a backdoor draw that becomes a primary draw on the turn; a two-pair
  redraw to a full house that blocks the opponent's flush; counting hidden outs
  is the difference between a 14% equity call and a 22% equity call — the player
  who counts only obvious outs consistently over-folds in close spots
- Street changes: the flop, turn, and river each change the board and the
  relative strength of every hand; a flush draw on the flop that misses the
  turn loses value; a paired board on the turn changes both players' ranges
- Relative hand strength vs absolute hand strength: absolute hand strength is
  how strong your hand is in isolation regardless of context — top pair top
  kicker is a strong hand; relative hand strength is how strong your hand is
  against the specific villain's range given all available information — top
  pair top kicker is a weak hand when the villain's range is overwhelmingly
  full of two pairs, sets, and straights given three streets of heavy action;
  beginners think in absolute terms and can't fold top pair; intermediate players
  learn relative hand strength and can fold the best possible hand they believed
  they had; the question to ask is not "is my hand strong?" but "is my hand
  strong enough relative to what this opponent is likely to have here?";
  relative hand strength determines whether a hand is for value, bluff catching,
  or folding — the same hand can be all three depending on opponent and action

Typical drills:
- "Board: 9s-8s-2h. Name every draw possible. Who benefits from this board,
  the preflop raiser or caller? Why?"
- "You hold Kd-Qd on a Jd-Td-4c board. How many outs do you have? What is
  your approximate equity using the rule of 4?"
- "The pot is 100, opponent bets 100. You have a flush draw and a backdoor
  straight draw. What is your total approximate equity? Is this a call?"

What the learner can do after W6:
- Instantly classify any flop as dry, wet, connected, or two-tone
- Count outs for any standard draw and convert to approximate equity
- Understand why betting a draw can be correct even when it loses

### Block 2 — Context And Range (W7–W11)
#### Ceiling: confident recreational

**W7: Range Thinking Lite**

Cognitive shift: opponents do not hold one specific hand — they hold a range
of hands consistent with all their actions so far. The learner who thinks in
ranges sees the whole picture; the learner who thinks about one hand guesses.

Core concepts:
- The range concept: before seeing the cards, any opponent holds a distribution
  of hands; the best decision considers the whole distribution, not one guess
- Putting opponents on a range: use position, action, sizing, and board to
  narrow the range at each street
- Range buckets: strong made hands, medium made hands, draws, weak hands,
  total air — sorting opponent actions into these buckets
- Board fitness: how well does a flop connect with the opponent's likely range?
  An UTG open-raiser's range hits A-K-J very well; it hits 7-8-9 poorly
- Hand combination counting: not all hands appear equally often;
  AK has 16 possible combinations (4 aces × 4 kings); a pocket pair (e.g., QQ)
  has only 6 combinations (C(4,2)); knowing combination counts prevents
  over-weighting unlikely holdings when building a mental range;
  KK has 6 combos; AKs has 4 combos; AKo has 12 combos
- Updating the range: each action opponent takes removes hands from their range
  and promotes others; a check on the river by a tight player usually removes
  most value hands from their range
- Range vs range equity: your decision is not "does my hand beat their hand?"
  but "does my hand profit against all the hands in their range, weighted by
  frequency?"

Typical drills:
- "UTG opens and CBets on K-7-2 rainbow. What hands are in their range?
  What hands are now unlikely given the cbet?"
- "How many combos of AA are there? AKo? KQs? Why does the combo count matter
  when deciding how often to expect a specific strong hand?"
- "Villain calls your flop bet, then leads the turn. What does this tell you
  about their range? Name three hands that become more likely."

What the learner can do after W7:
- Think about any opponent as holding a range, not a single hand
- Count approximate combination frequencies for common hands
- Narrow a villain's range at each street using their actions as filters

**W8: Stack Depth And Risk**

Cognitive shift: the size of the stacks relative to the pot changes what hands
are worth playing and how committed you should be. Deeper stacks mean more risk,
more reward, and more postflop complexity.

Core concepts:
- Stack-to-pot ratio (SPR) revisited at the decision level: a high SPR means
  more streets to play and more room to maneuver; a low SPR means you are
  close to all-in and commitment decisions dominate
- Effective stack: the smaller of the two stacks; the effective stack determines
  maximum risk in any hand; a 200bb stack playing vs a 50bb stack has an
  effective stack of 50bb — no one can win or lose more than 50bb
- Stack sizes and hand playability: deep stacks (150bb+) increase the value
  of implied-odds hands like small pocket pairs and suited connectors;
  short stacks (below 40bb) reduce implied odds value and increase the value
  of hands that play well as shoves or calls
- Commitment threshold: at what SPR does calling a bet commit you to going
  all the way? SPR of 1 means any call is effectively a commit; SPR of 5
  still leaves multiple streets before commitment
- Format differences — 6-max vs full ring: the same hand plays differently
  at a full-ring (9-player) vs 6-max (6-player) table; UTG at full ring
  opens only premium hands because 8 players remain; UTG at 6-max opens
  significantly wider because only 5 players remain; this is the first
  explicit format-awareness concept in the curriculum
- Short-stack strategy (below 40bb): the postflop decision tree compresses;
  most hands become raise-or-fold preflop and shove-or-fold postflop;
  set-mining with small pairs is incorrect below 15x implied odds (i.e.,
  below ~15bb effective stack); the strategic simplification of short stacks
  has both benefits and costs
- Risk management: playing a $200 buy-in at $1/$2 vs a $50 buy-in changes
  the financial risk and emotional pressure; matching buy-in size to bankroll
  and emotional comfort is a practical skill separate from technical play

Typical drills:
- "You have 200bb. Villain has 30bb. What is the effective stack? How does this
  change how you play AA preflop?"
- "SPR is 2 on the flop. You have top pair. What hands can you fold?
  What does the low SPR tell you about your decision?"
- "You are UTG at a 9-handed table with 100bb. Your range is tight. The same
  player moves to UTG at a 6-max table. How does their opening range change?"

What the learner can do after W8:
- Calculate effective stack for any two players at the table
- Adjust hand selection based on stack depth and SPR
- Name the key strategic differences between full ring and 6-max

**W9: Tournament Pressure**

Cognitive shift: tournament poker is not cash poker with more players.
The chips have different value — and survival matters. The player who treats
tournaments like cash games bleeds equity at every critical spot.

Core concepts:
- Tournament vs cash: in cash, every chip is worth its face value; in a
  tournament, the value of chips decreases as you accumulate more because
  you cannot remove them as cash — eliminating a short stack is worth more
  than the chips gained
- Survival value: being alive in a tournament has economic value independent
  of chip count; a 50% equity shove that busts you might be -EV in a tournament
  even though it would be exactly break-even in cash
- M-ratio (Dan Harrington): current stack ÷ (SB + BB + antes); M < 10 means
  emergency mode; M < 5 means push/fold territory; M gives a rough measure
  of how many orbits you can survive without playing a hand
- Bubble pressure: near the money bubble, short stacks tighten up to survive;
  big stacks can exploit this by raising frequently; medium stacks are caught
  between aggression and safety
- Risk premium: near pay jumps, the EV of a coin flip decreases because
  busting is significantly more costly than doubling up is beneficial —
  this is the first introduction to ICM logic without calling it ICM
- Stack size relative to blinds: a 20bb stack at 100/200 plays very differently
  from a 20bb stack at 25/50; absolute chip count is less important than
  stack-to-blind ratio
- Push/fold basics: when the stack falls below ~15bb, most hands become shove
  or fold — the postflop decision tree effectively disappears and preflop
  hand equity vs calling ranges determines the correct action

Typical drills:
- "You have 8,000 chips. Blinds are 200/400 with 50 antes. What is your M-ratio?
  Are you in push/fold territory?"
- "You are on the bubble with 18bb. A 40bb player raises from CO. You hold
  A8o on the BTN. Why might this be a fold when it would be a call in cash?"
- "A short stack shoves and you have 99. In a cash game this is a call.
  Why might you fold this near a pay jump?"

What the learner can do after W9:
- Calculate M-ratio and know what threshold demands push/fold play
- Recognize bubble dynamics and adjust range accordingly
- Explain in plain terms why a +chip EV play can be –$ EV in a tournament

**W10: Player Adjustment**

Cognitive shift: exploiting opponents is more profitable than playing a fixed
strategy against everyone. Recognizing player types and adjusting in real time
is the primary source of edge at micro and low stakes.

Core concepts:
- Player type classification: loose (plays many hands) vs tight (plays few);
  passive (rarely bets/raises) vs aggressive (bets and raises frequently);
  the four archetypes — loose-passive (calling station), loose-aggressive (LAG),
  tight-passive (nit), tight-aggressive (TAG)
- Exploiting the calling station: stop bluffing; increase value bet frequency;
  size up with strong hands; never slow play; bet every street for value with
  any hand that beats their calling range
- Exploiting the nit: steal their blinds frequently; fold to their raises unless
  you have premium hands; never pay off a tight player's large bets
- Exploiting the LAG: tighten your calling range vs their wide opens; trap with
  strong hands; check-call more; let them bluff into your strong hands
- Micro-stakes specific exploits: the most common micro-stakes player profile
  is loose-passive; they over-limp preflop, call too wide against CBets, play
  fit-or-fold (bet when they hit, check-fold when they miss), and fold to any
  sustained aggression on later streets; recognizing this profile means the
  primary adjustment is: bet for value relentlessly, reduce bluff frequency,
  and never slow play strong hands
- Fit-or-fold recognition: players who check-fold the turn after missing the
  flop reveal their entire postflop strategy; against them, double-barreling
  on any turn card that could plausibly improve your range is highly profitable
- Note-taking: in online poker, recording opponent tendencies after observing
  them is the mechanism by which table reads persist beyond a single hand;
  even a simple note ("folds turn to 2nd barrel 80%") is worth significant EV

Typical drills:
- "You identify a player with 42/3 stats (VPIP 42%, PFR 3%). What type is this?
  How does this change your strategy vs them?"
- "Villain calls your flop CBet on K-7-2. Turn is 3. They check. You have 98o
  (a complete miss). Do you barrel? Why?"
- "Name the three most common micro-stakes leaks and the specific adjustment
  you make for each one."

What the learner can do after W10:
- Classify any opponent into one of the four archetypes within 15 minutes
- Make specific, named adjustments against each type
- Stop applying the same strategy to all opponents regardless of their play style

**W11: Real Play Transfer / Capstone**

Cognitive shift: knowing poker concepts and executing them under live pressure
are different skills. The capstone is not more theory — it is the full mental
and practical readiness check before the learner enters real play with money on
the line.

Core concepts:
- Translating learned concepts to live decisions: range thinking, position
  awareness, pot odds, and board reading must happen simultaneously in real
  time; the first sessions will feel slow and deliberate — this is correct
  and temporary
- Pre-session checklist: stack sizes, player types (first 15 minutes of
  observation before playing hands), and mental state; never start a session
  when tilted, tired, or distracted — the decision quality required to win
  is degraded in all three states
- The decision-making process: before every action, ask three questions —
  "What is villain's likely range?", "What does my range look like to villain?",
  "What is my objective with this action (value/bluff/protect)?"
- Variance is installed here: the full mental primer from W12's content
  is seeded at this capstone; correct play loses regularly; one session is
  not signal; tilt is a real phenomenon with a known cause and known fix
- Session journaling: after each real-money session, write 3 lines —
  one hand you played well, one hand you played poorly, and one pattern
  you noticed about your own tendencies; this loop compounds faster than
  passive playing alone
- Identifying table conditions: which seat is most profitable today? Who is
  the weakest player? Who should you avoid? First 15 minutes is for observation,
  not hero plays

Typical drills:
- "You sit down at a new table. What do you observe in the first 10 hands before
  playing your first significant pot? Name three things you are looking for."
- "Walk through a complete hand decision process: preflop range estimate,
  position check, flop action, turn action. Do it in under 60 seconds."
- "You lost two buy-ins in your first real session. Walk through the mental
  checklist: was this variance, was it a strategy error, or was it tilt?"

What the learner can do after W11:
- Sit down in a real game with a structured pre-session and in-hand process
- Identify table conditions and player types from observation
- Survive their first bad session without misdiagnosing correct play as broken
- Begin building a session journal habit that compounds into a personal coaching loop

### Block 2.5 — Mindset Bridge (W12)
#### Ceiling: variance-proof player

**W12: Variance, Decisions, And The Player's Mindset**

Cognitive shift: poker has a noise layer that is impossible to remove. Correct
decisions lose regularly. Incorrect decisions win regularly. The player who
confuses outcomes with decisions will always mislearn — fixing things that are
not broken and repeating mistakes that happened to win.

This world is placed here deliberately: the learner has survived W1–W11 and
is about to enter postflop complexity. Before that door opens, the variance
lens must be fully installed.

Core concepts:
- EV explained: expected value as the long-run average of a decision, not one outcome
- Variance defined: the natural spread of results around EV — larger in poker
  than in almost any other skill game
- Outcome vs decision quality: the only thing you control is the quality of
  the decision; the result is managed by probability
- Sample size intuition: one session is noise; 10,000 hands begins to be signal
- Downswing expectation math: at a real win rate of 5bb/100, a downswing of
  150–250bb over 20,000–40,000 hands falls within the normal statistical range;
  this means a winning player can run bad for 3–5 months of regular online
  volume without any strategic error; the math of poker variance is genuinely
  worse than most players expect before seeing the numbers; knowing the expected
  downswing for your format is the single best protection against premature
  strategy changes or quitting during a normal negative run
- Bad beat vs bad play: how to tell the difference after a hand
- Tilt mechanics: how a bad outcome degrades the next decision if unaddressed
- The "results-oriented trap": changing a correct strategy because of
  a short losing run is the most common way skilled players regress
- Bankroll as variance buffer: why you need more buy-ins than feels necessary
- The player's identity: "I make correct decisions" not "I win sessions"

Typical drills:
- "You call a pot-odds-correct draw on the flop. The turn and river brick.
Was this a mistake?" (Answer: no — walk through the math.)
- "You lost 4 buy-ins in two sessions. List the questions you ask to
  determine whether your strategy needs fixing or your sample is just noise."
- "What is the minimum number of hands before a winrate estimate is meaningful?
  Why does this number feel uncomfortably large?"

What the learner can do after W12:
- Stop misdiagnosing correct decisions as mistakes after bad outcomes
- Recognize tilt triggers before acting on them
- Describe variance to another player without using the word "luck"
- Begin the next technical block (postflop) with a stable mental model instead
  of a results-driven one

### Block 3 — Postflop (W13–W16)
#### Ceiling: winning micro/small stakes

**W13: Postflop Range Construction**

Cognitive shift: postflop decisions are not about your two cards — they are
about how your whole range lands on this board vs the opponent's range.

Core concepts:
- Range advantage: who has more strong hands on this texture?
- Equity vs range: your hand's equity against a realistic opponent range
- Nut advantage: who has the best possible hands more often?
- Blocking: holding a card that reduces opponent combinations of strong hands
- Equity realization: raw equity and realized equity differ — a hand's raw
  equity (e.g., 55% on the flop) is not automatically captured; position,
  playability, and ability to apply pressure determine how much of that
  equity is actually converted into chips; AK in position realizes ~95%+
  of its equity; AK out of position realizes significantly less because
  of check-call limitations and inability to bet all streets; suited connectors
  out of position realize less equity than in position even with identical
  raw percentages; equity realization explains WHY solvers recommend tighter
  OOP calling ranges despite seemingly sufficient raw equity numbers
- Backdoor draws: a hand with a backdoor flush or straight draw (requiring two running cards to complete) has ~4% additional equity per backdoor draw — these small equity additions accumulate and regularly convert a marginal flop fold into a correct call or check-call; ignoring backdoor equity systematically over-folds in close spots
- Intro to continuation betting as a range tool, not a hand tool
- Initiative as a named concept: the player who made the last aggressive action
  (bet or raise) preflop has the initiative postflop — the right to represent
  strength and make the first bet without it looking suspicious; the player
  without initiative (who called preflop) must check more often or use donk bets
  to reclaim aggression; initiative transfers when the preflop caller check-raises
  or leads into the aggressor; understanding initiative explains why CBets are
  profitable even with weak hands (the bettor represents their entire preflop range),
  why calling stations destroy initiative value, and why check-raising is the
  primary tool for the preflop caller to reclaim equity leadership

Typical drills: "Which player has range advantage on A-K-2 rainbow? Who has
nut advantage on 7-8-9?" Recognition of who should be the aggressor by range.

**W14: Bet Sizing As Message**

Cognitive shift: every size sends a specific message. Bigger sizes polarize;
smaller sizes are merged. The learner must start asking "what does this size
say about my range?"

Core concepts:
- Polarized sizing: big bets with strong hands and bluffs, fold out medium
- Merged sizing: small bets with a wide, medium-strength range
- SPR (stack-to-pot ratio): how SPR controls commitment and sizing decisions
- Low SPR commitment framework: when SPR is 1–3, the hand is commit-or-fold rather than decision-based; with an SPR of 2 and top pair, checking for pot control is rarely correct — you will be pot-committed regardless; the practical rule is: if you are willing to call a shove, lead; if not, fold; understanding SPR threshold for commitment vs escape is one of the highest-frequency practical heuristics in postflop play
- Geometric bet sizing on multi-street play
- Pot geometry (multi-street stack planning): before betting the flop,
  decide whether you want stacks in on the river; if yes, work backwards:
  a pot-sized bet on flop leaves SPR ~1 on turn, forcing commitment;
  a half-pot flop bet may leave too much behind and allow escape;
  the professional question is not just "what size for this street?" but
  "what is the sizing sequence that gets the right hands into the pot for
  all the chips?"; pot geometry is how value bets and large bluffs are
  constructed to be geometrically sound rather than improvised street by street
- Reading opponent sizing patterns as range signals

Typical drills: "You have top pair on a dry board, SPR = 3. What size fits your
range best?" Size selection with reasoning about message and range.

**W15: Flop Strategy**

Cognitive shift: the flop decision is not just "do I bet?" — it is "does my
whole range benefit from betting, and what size serves my range?"

Core concepts:
- CBet frequency vs texture: when to bet often vs selectively
- Check-back with strong hands: protection vs deception
- Board coverage: betting to represent a wide range, not just strong hands
- Protection betting: thin value + preventing free equity for opponent draws
- Slowplay theory: trapping with very strong hands (sets, top two pair on dry
  boards) is correct when the board is unlikely to give the opponent free
  equity, the opponent will bluff or bet themselves, and the pot is not yet
  large enough to demand protection; slowplay is wrong when the board is wet
  (opponent can draw out cheaply), when the opponent is unlikely to bluff
  or bet, or when you are out of position and cannot control the pot;
  the default should be to bet strong hands for protection and value;
  slowplay is a specific, earned deviation from that default, not a style choice
- Equity denial: betting specifically to force folds and reduce opponent equity
  realization — distinct from thin value; the goal is not to get called, but to
  prevent the opponent from seeing another card for free
- Texture categories: monotone, paired, dry, connected — and how they shift CBet logic
- Monotone flop strategy: when the flop is three of a suit, the preflop raiser's
  range contains fewer suited hands proportionally; check-back more, use smaller
  sizes, OOP player can lead more frequently
- Paired board strategy: when the board pairs (K-K-2, 7-7-3), trips are rare in
  both ranges; big bets become more credible as representation of trips; the PFR
  retains range advantage but must size correctly to extract
- Donk bet basics: when leading into the aggressor has merit
- Check-raise: using a check followed by a raise to build pots with strong hands,
  deny equity to draws, or semi-bluff — the most powerful flop counter-move
- Polarized flop raising vs merged flop raising: when you face a CBet and raise,
  the raise range is a critical strategic decision; a polarized raising range
  contains only very strong made hands (sets, two pairs) and semi-bluffs (combo
  draws, flush draws with equity) — nothing in between; the advantage: opponent
  cannot profitably call with medium-strength hands because they are crushed or
  drawing thin; a merged raising range contains a continuous band of hands
  including strong top pairs, good draws, and strong made hands — the advantage
  is balance but the disadvantage is giving opponent better calling odds with
  medium hands; in practice, check-raises on wet boards should lean polarized
  (semi-bluffs + nuts) because the board gives both players equity and a merged
  raise bleeds value; check-raises on dry boards can be slightly more merged
  because the board favors made hands and fewer draws exist; the naming
  distinction matters because it determines which hands belong in the raising
  range and which belong in the calling range — without it, players construct
  incoherent check-raise ranges that are both bluff-heavy enough to be called
  down and value-light enough to be unexploitable in the wrong direction

Typical drills: "K-3-2 rainbow as preflop raiser vs caller. Range advantage
or not? Bet whole range or select?" Strategic flop frequency decisions.

**W16: Turn And River Commitment**

Cognitive shift: by the turn, commitments are being made. The learner must
understand when a hand is too strong to give up and when to abort a bluff.

Core concepts:
- Double barrel: when does the turn improve your hand or your narrative?
- Give up spots: turn cards that kill your bluff story or equity
- MDF (minimum defense frequency): how often you must call to stop pure bluffs
- Thin value on the river: betting mediocre hands for thin value vs checking
  for showdown
- River jam vs smaller bet: sizing choice as a commitment signal
- The concept of being "pot committed" vs playing off SPR
- Float play: calling the flop with a weak hand or draw, intending to take
  the pot on the turn when the original aggressor shows weakness
- Free card play: checking or under-betting on a street where you are the
  draw or marginal hand, specifically to see the next card cheaply; IP players
  can call a flop bet and check back the turn to take a free river; the
  counter to free card play is the free card bet — betting small in position
  as the draw to buy a cheap turn card if called; granting or denying free
  cards is a strategic dimension of postflop decision-making distinct from
  value-betting and bluffing, particularly important in multi-street draw spots
- Probe bet: betting the turn after the preflop aggressor checks back the flop —
  exploiting the range weakness their check-back signals
- Turn equity lead (turn nudging): when you called the flop as the non-aggressor,
  your range on the turn sometimes has more overall equity than the PFR's range
  even without more nut hands — in these spots you can lead the turn to realize
  your equity advantage and prevent the PFR from getting a free card with their
  weaker betting range
- Board runout analysis: before acting on the turn, consider what each possible
  run-out card does to both players' ranges — a flush-completing card dramatically
  shifts equity toward the player with suited hands; a pairing card devalues
  two-pair and draw hands; a low blank may reinforce or reverse range advantage;
  this card-by-card range-shift analysis prevents turn and river mistakes caused
  by ignoring how the board texture evolved
- Delayed continuation bet: checking back the flop as the preflop aggressor,
  then betting the turn — not a double barrel (which requires a flop bet), but
  a deliberate check-then-bet sequence; used to protect a check-back range,
  trap opponents who lead turns weakly, and extract value from boards that
  improve more on the turn than the flop; a delayed CBet on a turn card that
  connects with the PFR's range is particularly powerful because it reads as
  strong after a check that appeared passive
- Turn overbet: betting larger than the pot on the turn to apply simultaneous
  pressure on both the turn call and the remaining river stack; a turn overbet
  reduces the remaining SPR to near 1, forcing the opponent to commit or fold;
  most effective when the bettor has nut advantage on a specific runout and wants
  to deny equity while building a pot for a large river bet; distinct from river
  overbet — the goal is range compression and pot control, not final polarization
- Block bet (defensive small bet OOP): betting very small out of position on
  the river (typically 20–25% pot) specifically to reach showdown cheaply and
  prevent the IP player from betting large; a block bet with a medium-strength
  hand sacrifices some value but controls the pot; most effective with hands that
  beat bluffs but lose to the IP player's value range — the alternative of
  check-call often costs more against a larger IP sizing

Typical drills: "You CBet the flop with a flush draw. The turn bricks and
you have no pair. Double barrel or give up? Why?" Multi-street line decisions.

### Block 4 — Advanced Tactics And Meta (W17–W21)
#### Ceiling: stable regular / small-to-mid stakes

**W17: 3-Bet Dynamics**

Cognitive shift: 3-betting is not just "I have AA." It is a range tool that
generates dead money, applies positional pressure, and balances your defending
range.

Core concepts:
- Value 3-bets vs bluff/light 3-bets: building a balanced re-raise range
- Positional 3-betting: IP 3-bets are more profitable and wider
- Squeeze plays: 3-betting after a call to isolate with dead money
- 4-bet bluffing: the necessary counter-balance to a 3-bet heavy approach
- Defending vs 3-bets: when to call, 4-bet, or fold to a re-raise
- Cold calling a 3-bet (as the original raiser): distinct from re-raising;
  when stack depth, position, hand playability, and opponent tendencies
  make calling the 3-bet better than 4-betting or folding; cold calling
  is more appropriate when IP, when the opponent 3-bets too wide for a
  4-bet to be profitable, or when your hand has high playability but
  not enough equity to commit 4-bet stacks; a cold call creates a single-raised
  pot dynamic on the flop rather than a 3-bet pot dynamic, giving the
  caller more flexibility — but requires post-flop skill to realize the
  equity advantage
- 3-bet sizing by position: OOP 3-bets should be larger (3.5x–4.5x the open)
  to compensate for positional disadvantage postflop; IP 3-bets can be smaller
  (2.5x–3.5x) because position allows more flexibility post-flop even with
  a smaller pot; live 3-bets are typically larger than online due to the
  different effective stack-to-pot dynamics at live tables; sizing OOP vs IP
  is a standard adjustment that changes the frequency of profitable calls
  the opponent can make against you
- Limp-reraise trap: limping with a premium hand (AA, KK, occasionally QQ/AK)
  to trap an aggressive player into raising, then reraising; most effective
  when: you are OOP against an aggressive BTN who attacks limps frequently;
  the raise size you will face is predictable; your hand plays well for stacks;
  a limp-reraise communicates a narrow, strong range — calling becomes very
  difficult for the opponent even with a good hand; overusing this play
  makes your limp range readable and exploitable
- Stack depth and 3-bet sizing: deeper stacks support larger 3-bets
- 3-bet pot postflop play: both players arrive at the flop with stronger,
  narrower ranges and higher SPR commitment; CBet frequencies, sizing, and
  check-back decisions all change vs single-raised pots
- In 3-bet pots IP as the PFR: high-frequency small CBet on many textures due
  to range advantage; check back to induce on dry boards
- In 3-bet pots OOP as the caller: range is more capped; large donk-bets are
  rare; check-call or check-raise are the main tools
- 4-bet pot postflop play: in a 4-bet pot both players have extremely narrow
  value-heavy ranges; the flop SPR is often near 1–2 which means most hands
  are effectively committed; standard CBet frequencies and sizing logic do
  not apply — near-pot or larger bets are the correct default size because
  the pot is already large and range compression is complete; checking back
  in a 4-bet pot is a strong trapping signal because players rarely check
  top-of-range hands; 4-bet pots remove most of the postflop decision
  complexity because the ranges are so narrow and the stacks are so short
  relative to the pot that nearly every strong hand is a commit-or-fold spot

Typical drills: "You hold A5s on BTN vs UTG open. Three players in the pot.
Is this a squeeze candidate? What range do you use?"

**W18: Multiway Pots**

Cognitive shift: multiway pots require range compression — you need stronger
hands to bet for value because equity is split and someone always has a piece.

Core concepts:
- Range compression: narrowing to strong hands in multiway spots
- Checking back strong hands for protection and pot control
- Side pots: how side pots form and what they mean for decision-making
- Multiway texture reading: who benefits from a wet board in a 3-way pot?
- Bluff frequency reduction: bluffing multiple opponents requires fold equity
  from all of them
- Position advantage in multiway pots: IP player controls pot size

Typical drills: "You flop top pair in a 3-way pot out of position. Bet, check,
or lead? Why does your hand need a different treatment than heads up?"

**W19: Balanced vs Exploitative**

Cognitive shift: GTO is a theoretical ceiling; exploitation is how money is
actually made against imperfect opponents. The learner must understand when
to deviate from balance and when to stay near it.

Core concepts:
- GTO conceptual overview: equilibrium, indifference, unexploitability
- The Fundamental Theorem of Poker: every time you play a hand differently
  from how you would play it if you could see all opponent cards, they gain;
  every time you play it the same way you would with full information, they
  lose; this is David Sklansky's core insight and the conceptual engine behind
  GTO and exploitative play simultaneously — GTO minimizes the times your
  opponent gains this advantage over you; exploitation maximizes the times you
  gain it over them; understanding the theorem changes the framing from
  "what hand do I have?" to "what information advantage is at stake right now?"
- Population tendencies: what does the average player at your stakes do wrong?
- How to identify exploitable patterns: call-too-often, fold-too-often,
  size tells, predictable lines
- Risk of over-exploiting: smart opponents adjust, turning exploits into leaks
- Mixed strategies: why solvers mix, and when humans can simplify to a pure line
- Game theory applied: when to protect your range vs when to max-exploit
- Self-balance audit: can your opponent exploit YOU? Checking your own strategy
  for systematic over-folds, over-calls, or predictable sizings — being
  exploitable is as costly as failing to exploit; the audit question is
  "if villain knew my strategy, what would they do differently?"
- Capped range exploitation: when one player's range cannot contain the strongest
  hands (e.g., they checked back the flop as PFR), the other player can attack
  with high frequency — the capped player cannot raise for value, only call
- Attacking the capped range: sizing up, bluffing at higher frequency, denying
  showdown to medium-strength hands that have no protection
- Population-adjusted exploitation: using a solver against a population that
  heavily deviates from GTO produces misleading output; the correct approach
  is to approximate the actual population's strategy first, then solve for the
  best counter; at micro and low stakes, populations over-fold to 3-bets,
  call too wide on draws, and under-bluff rivers — a strategy optimized
  against a GTO opponent is systematically sub-optimal against this population;
  the W19 lesson: GTO is the ceiling, but your actual edge comes from modeling
  opponent deviations accurately, not from playing GTO in a field that doesn't
- Zoom and fast-fold poker: on anonymous fast-fold tables no player history
  accumulates between hands — this removes the exploitation layer and shifts
  the optimal strategy closer to GTO; wider balanced ranges and less targeted
  exploitation are correct adjustments; HUD data becomes sparse and less
  actionable; the anonymous-pool baseline strategy is a real-world use case
  for the balanced side of the W19 spectrum

Typical drills: "Villain folds to river bets 80% of the time. You have a missed
draw. Do you bluff more, less, or the same as GTO? Why?"

**W20: Advanced Reads And Exploit**

Cognitive shift: live tells and online timing patterns are real information.
The learner learns to systematically log and use them instead of reacting
instinctively.

Core concepts:
- Timing tells online: snap-calls indicate strength or draws, delays indicate
  decisions with marginal hands
- Sizing patterns: players often use consistent bet sizes for strong vs bluff hands
- Leveling: first-level (own hand), second-level (opponent's hand), third-level
  (what opponent thinks you have)
- Live tells: eye contact, chip handling, speech play, acting strong vs weak
- Building a read: confirmation bias trap — one data point is not a read
- Bet sizing tells on rivers: large overbets often indicate either nuts or air
- Table image: your perceived playing style affects how opponents respond —
  a tight image makes bluffs more credible; a loose image earns more value calls
- Isolation raise strategy: raising to get heads-up with a specific weak player;
  the goal is not just to "play vs limpers" but to target a recreational player
  specifically — raise from any position with a wider-than-normal range to
  build a pot IP vs a player who plays fit-or-fold, calls too wide preflop,
  and cannot defend appropriately postflop; the correct isolation sizing is
  larger vs multiple limpers (to price out other callers); isolation is the
  primary mechanism by which player-type reads translate directly into EV;
  every hand played HU vs the weakest player at the table is worth more than
  a hand vs a regular even with a slightly worse holding
- Table ecology and recreational player management: your edge at a live cash
  game comes almost entirely from the recreational players; keeping them
  comfortable, not embarrassing them when they make mistakes, not angle-shooting,
  and not doing anything that might cause them to leave or rebuy elsewhere is
  directly profitable; "don't educate the fish" is an EV concept, not just
  courtesy; discussing bad beats sympathetically, avoiding visible frustration,
  and maintaining a relaxed atmosphere keeps recreational players at the table
  longer and makes them willing to reload; the player who chases a fish off the
  table with arrogance loses a long-term revenue source
- Real-time hand reading: systematically narrowing villain's range street by
  street during live play, not in study; before the flop: "UTG opens — what
  is their range?"; on the flop, each action updates the range — a check by
  a tight player removes most strong hands; a large bet eliminates air;
  by the river, villain's remaining range is a filtered subset of their
  preflop range modified by every action; players who ask "what is villain's
  range right now?" on every street make fundamentally better decisions than
  players who only think about their own hand; this is the live, in-hand
  version of the range thinking skill first introduced in W7
- Time bank usage as strategic signal (online): using a full time bank before
  a river call or fold signals a genuine decision; snap-folding often indicates
  no continuation interest; snap-calling on the river with a large bet often
  indicates a nutted hand or a read; skilled players exploit predictable
  time-bank patterns as an additional information layer alongside HUD stats
- Metagame: how hand history between two players shifts optimal future strategy —
  a bluff you showed changes their calling frequency for the rest of the session

Typical drills: "Villain always uses 1/3 pot on flush-draw boards but 3/4 pot
on dry boards. What does this tell you about their river range?"

**W21: Session Mastery And Growth**

Cognitive shift: results in a single session are noise. A professional
evaluates process quality, not outcome, and builds sustainable study habits
over time.

*Note: This world covers session-level game selection — recognizing a bad
game mid-session and making the decision to leave. Stake-selection discipline
and bankroll management are covered at the professional level in W27.*

Core concepts:
- Tilt identification: emotional triggers, early signs, recovery protocols
- Variance acceptance at the session level: bad sessions happen to good players
- Session structure: warm-up, play, review loop
- Table selection within a session: recognizing a bad game and leaving it
- Stopping rules: time-based vs condition-based session endings
- Stop-win discipline: leaving a session while ahead is a legitimate professional
  tool — not because locking in results is strategically correct (all sessions
  are part of the same infinite game) but because winning sessions are a common
  tilt trigger in reverse; winner's tilt causes loose, speculative play that
  erodes the session profit; a stop-win rule of "leave after doubling the buy-in
  if you notice your decisions getting sloppy" is a practical protection; the
  underlying principle is: stop when decision quality drops, not when results
  reach a number — but for learners who cannot yet self-diagnose decision quality,
  a fixed stop-win threshold is a reasonable substitute
- Fancy play syndrome (FPS): choosing a complex, clever line over a simple,
  profitable one because the complex line feels more satisfying or shows off
  skill; examples include check-raising a hand that should simply bet for value,
  slowplaying when betting protects better, or using a multi-street bluff when
  a single-barrel has higher EV; FPS costs EV because it sacrifices profitable
  simplicity for unnecessary complexity; the antidote is to always ask "does
  the clever line actually earn more than the obvious line?" before deviating;
  FPS is the most common leak in players who have learned too much theory but
  have not yet calibrated it to real opponent populations
- Reciprocality: Tommy Angelo's insight that exploits only work because the
  opponent has corresponding leaks — if you gain EV by bluffing too much,
  they lose the same EV by calling too little; this means that recognizing
  your own weaknesses and your opponents' is a symmetric skill; the extension
  of reciprocality: knowing when YOU are the bad player at the table today;
  ego and fatigue can make you the fish in a specific session even at a stake
  you usually beat; the hardest strategic decision in poker is leaving a game
  because you are not your best self, even when you believe you belong at
  that table on average
- Studying from sessions: how to tag hands, review leaks, prioritize fixes

Typical drills: "You lost three buy-ins in a session. What are the first three
things you check before deciding whether to stop or continue?"

### Block 5 — Solver And Theory (W22–W25)
#### Ceiling: thinking professional

**W22: Solver Introduction**

Cognitive shift: solvers do not "know the answer" — they find unexploitable
frequencies. Understanding what a solver solves changes how you frame decisions.

Core concepts:
- What is a game tree? Nodes, actions, terminal nodes
- Nash equilibrium: what it means and why it matters
- Frequency language: "bet 60% / check 40%" — what this actually means at a table
- Mixed strategies: why a solver sometimes bets and sometimes checks the same hand
- Solver vs exploitative thinking: when to apply each
- How to run a basic solver query: board, ranges, position inputs
- Solver simplification into practical heuristics: raw solver output ("bet 63%,
  check 37%") is not directly usable at a table; the professional workflow is
  to study a family of similar spots and extract a simple rule — "always bet
  K-high dry boards in position," "check back A-x boards vs passive callers"
  — this heuristic extraction process is how solver knowledge becomes in-game
  strategy; the goal is not to memorize frequencies but to own the logic behind
  them well enough to derive a confident decision in real time
- Multi-street game planning: before committing chips, think through multiple
  action trees — "if I bet and get called, what do I do on each turn card?"
  "if I bet and get raised, am I prepared to continue or fold?"; this preemptive
  planning before the action is Phil Galfond's "game planning" framework and is
  one of the most impactful habits that separates professional-level thinking
  from reactive decision-making; game planning prevents being surprised by
  runouts and makes multi-street bluffs coherent instead of improvised

Typical drills: "On a K-7-2 rainbow board, the solver checks back 45% of the
time with AK. Why? What does checking accomplish for the range?"

**W23: Preflop Range Construction**

Cognitive shift: preflop ranges are not hand lists — they are equilibrium
solutions for specific positions and stack depths. Learning to build and
internalize ranges is a professional discipline.

Core concepts:
- GTO open-raising ranges by position: UTG to BTN at 100bb
- Modern open sizing philosophy: old-school raises of 3x–4x have been
  replaced by 2x–2.5x in position in most online environments; a smaller
  open risks less while maintaining fold pressure; the BTN min-raise (2x)
  is a standard GTO opening size that allows a wide open range while risking
  only 2bb; UTG and EP opens are larger (2.5x–3x) because the range is tighter
  and the larger size is supported by hand strength; live poker still commonly
  uses 2.5x–4x because fewer hands per hour reduces the cost of larger sizing;
  learners who were taught 3x opens years ago are playing sub-optimally
  in modern online environments where 2–2.5x is standard
- 3-bet ranges: value + bluff composition by position
- 4-bet ranges and defending vs 4-bets
- Calling ranges: pot odds, equity, playability
- Stack depth adjustments: 50bb vs 100bb vs 150bb+ ranges differ significantly
- Range notation: how to read and write ranges in solver format
- SB limp range (modern GTO): modern solvers show SB should mix limps and
  raises rather than always raising or folding — limping the bottom of the
  open range and some speculative hands denies BB a simple response and
  allows SB to see cheap flops with the hands that play best that way;
  the practical implication: SB open-limping is not a mistake if done with
  the right hands at the right frequencies

Typical drills: "BTN opens 2.5x, SB 3-bets to 10bb. You are in the BB with
KJo at 100bb. What does the solver say, and why does stack depth matter here?"

**W24: River Theory**

Cognitive shift: on the river, no more cards come. Every action is a final
commitment. The learner must understand how polarized vs merged ranges, bluff
ratios, and pot odds create precise equilibrium on the last street.

Core concepts:
- Polarized river ranges: nuts and air, nothing in between
- Alpha: the threshold bet size at which opponent is indifferent to calling
- Optimal bluff frequency: value:bluff ratio to make opponent indifferent
- Indifference points: the exact call/fold equity threshold for each bet size
- Overbet theory: when an overbet is correct as the final range representation
- Checking rivers: which hands check for showdown vs bet for thin value
- River check-raise: distinct from flop check-raise; on the river a check-raise
  can only be a value raise or a pure bluff because there are no future streets;
  the river check-raise is the most powerful polarizing move — it communicates
  either the nuts or a very deliberate bluff with no middle ground possible;
  calling ranges vs river check-raise are tight because the raiser's range
  is maximally polarized; the river check-raise bluff requires blockers to
  the value range and an opponent who bets rivers widely enough for the play
  to have fold equity; without fold equity a river check-raise bluff loses
  to any calling hand; this tool is rarer and higher-commitment than flop
  or turn check-raises and its correct usage is an elite read-dependent play
- River thin value raise: when an opponent bets small on the river into your
  medium-strong hand, raising for thin value extracts extra chips from the
  bet-fold and bet-call segments of their range; the raise size should be
  moderate (2.5x–3x the bet) to keep bluff-catchers in; a thin value raise
  is incorrect when the opponent's betting range is too strong (raising into
  a range that beats you often), when a call extracts nearly as much value,
  or when the raise turns the hand into a pure bluff-catcher situation;
  the river thin value raise is distinct from the full-pot river jam — it
  is a small surgical extraction against opponents whose small river bets
  are wide and weak

Typical drills: "You bet 3/4 pot on the river with a polarized range. If you
have 6 value hands in your range, how many bluffs must you have for your
opponent to be indifferent to calling?"

**W25: Blocker Effects**

Cognitive shift: the cards in your hand affect opponent combinations directly.
The right bluff has blockers to the nuts; the wrong bluff unblocks opponent
calls.

Core concepts:
- Card removal: how holding a card changes opponent combos
- Nut blockers: holding the Ace of a flush suit reduces opponent nut flush combos
- Unblocking calls: a bluff that unblocks opponent calling hands is worse
- Blocker-aware river bluff selection: why A5o is often better than 67s as a bluff
- Board-specific blocker analysis: paired boards, flush boards, straight boards
- Range vs range blocker effects: how blockers shift overall equity distributions
- Card matching theory: in 3-bet pots, bluffing with hands that share the dominant
  suit of the board is more effective than unrelated bluffs — if the board is
  9c-7c-2c, holding a club reduces opponent nut-flush combos while giving your
  bluff a credible backdoor or made-flush narrative; selecting bluffs that
  'match' the board texture is a natural extension of blocker theory and
  specifically powerful in 3-bet pots where ranges are narrow and suit matching
  has a measurable impact on opponent combinations

Typical drills: "Board is Ah-Kh-Qh-2c-7d. You are choosing a river bluff.
Do you prefer Kd-Jd or Ac-5s? Walk through the blocker logic."

### Block 6 — Professional Tools (W26–W30)
#### Ceiling: working professional

**W26: ICM Mastery** *(MTT Track primary — Cash Track learners may skip or skim)*

Cognitive shift: in tournaments, chips do not have linear value. The chip
value decreases as the stack grows because of risk of elimination. ICM
math governs every significant tournament decision.

Core concepts:
- ICM explained: Independent Chip Model, chip EV vs dollar EV
- Bubble play: how ICM creates pressure near pay jumps
- Short-stack push/fold: Nash equilibrium push and call ranges by M-ratio
- Reshove ranges (3-bet shove at 12–30bb): when facing an open at 12–30bb,
  shoving all-in (reshove) is often correct and is categorically different from
  first-in shoving; reshove ranges include hands with sufficient equity to
  play for stacks while denying the original raiser positional advantage and
  reclaiming dead money; a reshove caller's range is narrower than an initial
  shove caller's range because the reshover represents a defined premium;
  at 15bb and below, folding facing an open and then folding facing a reshove
  are both costly leaks that compound rapidly in a tournament structure
- Skipping spots: when folding +cEV situations is correct due to ICM pressure
- Final table dynamics: stack distribution changes ICM equity dramatically
- Hand-for-hand play: near the money bubble, tournament directors announce
  hand-for-hand — each table plays exactly one hand simultaneously before
  starting the next; this prevents slow-playing the clock to wait for another
  table's elimination; it increases emotional pressure significantly and
  affects decision pace; knowing hand-for-hand is in effect changes nothing
  strategically — correct play does not change — but it does change mental
  pace and increases tilt risk for unprepared players
- ICM deal-making and chip-chop negotiations: at final tables, players often
  negotiate a deal to redistribute remaining prize pool before play concludes;
  the most common methods are ICM deal (each player receives their ICM equity
  share of the remaining prize pool) and chip-chop (split proportionally to
  chip counts); a professional negotiates from their exact ICM equity number,
  not from gut feel; common mistake is accepting less than ICM equity due to
  desire to close the event; leaving a small amount on the table for the
  winner preserves competitive incentive while reducing variance; knowing how
  to calculate your ICM equity accurately during negotiation is directly
  profitable at any live or online final table where deals occur
- Bubble abuse / big stack leverage: the most important tactical application of
  ICM from the other direction — a player with a large stack at the bubble can
  profitably shove a very wide range because medium and short stacks must
  over-fold to protect their ICM equity near pay jumps; they cannot call off
  their stack without an overwhelming equity edge; the big stack is immune to
  elimination risk and can exploit this asymmetry aggressively; correct bubble
  play as the big stack: widen shoving range significantly vs all non-big-stack
  opponents, especially the medium stack who is trying to ladder; target the
  shortest stacks last (they are actually more likely to call as they have less
  ICM to lose); when multiple short stacks are at risk simultaneously (hand for
  hand) the big stack abuse opportunity is at its maximum because nobody can
  risk elimination to defend against steals; understanding bubble abuse as the
  big stack is the active EV complement to understanding ICM caution as the short stack
- ICM mistakes: calling too wide at the bubble, over-shoving near pay jumps
- Progressive Knockout (PKO) tournaments: bounty EV is additive to regular EV;
  the value of knocking out a player includes their bounty prize; calling ranges
  widen when the bounty is large relative to the call size; the key formula:
  effective call price = call amount − (bounty value × probability of winning)
  — this creates spots where calls are +EV purely on bounty grounds even when
  chip EV says fold; PKO strategy diverges from standard ICM play especially
  near short stacks and bubbles
- Big blind ante (BBA) format: the overwhelming standard in modern online MTTs;
  instead of individual antes, the BB posts a large ante equal to one big blind;
  this enlarges pots and makes preflop opens more profitable; short-stack
  push/fold math changes because pot sizes are larger; BBA must be accounted
  for in any solver work that models tournament scenarios
- Mystery bounty tournaments: distinct from PKO — a fixed cash prize is sealed
  in an envelope revealed only when a specific player is eliminated; the bounty
  value cannot be calculated in advance and is not additive in the PKO sense;
  correct strategic adjustment is near-standard ICM play with a small EV
  premium for eliminating covered short stacks; calling ranges do NOT widen
  the same way as PKO because the expected bounty value is a lottery, not
  a deterministic number tied to bounty stack size
- Satellite strategy: the goal is to finish in the top-X qualifying seats,
  not to accumulate chips; once a player has enough chips to guarantee
  qualification (even if opponents play aggressively), additional chip
  accumulation has zero marginal EV; premium hands may correctly fold if
  an all-in risks elimination and you are currently chip-secure; calling
  ranges near the satellite bubble are dramatically tighter than standard
  ICM bubbles — the EV structure is binary (qualified / not qualified)
  rather than marginal
- ICM postflop: ICM pressure does not stop after the preflop decision;
  thin value bets and large bluffs near final table bubbles risk elimination
  for marginal upside; a bet that is +chip EV may be –$EV when ICM is applied;
  the heuristic: the closer a pay jump or seat qualification, the more
  conservative postflop commit decisions should be — large postflop bets
  and hero calls require higher equity to justify than in a standard cash game
- Re-entry and rebuy tournaments: during the re-entry or rebuy period,
  elimination has a reduced real cost — you can purchase another entry;
  correct adjustment is increased aggression for chip accumulation and
  willingness to accept higher-variance spots that would be incorrect folds
  in a freezeout; once the re-entry period closes, strategy reverts to
  standard freezeout ICM; important caution: chasing re-entries drains
  real dollar EV from session ROI — a re-entry is only correct if the
  expected EV of the new stack exceeds the re-entry cost after realistic
  edge estimation
- Turbo and hyper-turbo MTT structures: blind levels increase every 3–10
  minutes; this compresses the chip-EV phase and brings ICM pressure into
  play by level 2–4; push/fold and reshove ranges become relevant within
  the first 30–60 minutes of play; correct turbo strategy front-loads
  aggression, does not waste the chip-EV phase on passive play, and
  adjusts calling ranges wider because average stack depths are shallower
  throughout the entire event
- Stop-and-go play: at very short stacks (8–12bb) in tournament play,
  calling a preflop raise and then shoving the flop regardless of your
  cards or the flop texture can be more profitable than an immediate
  preflop shove; the stop-and-go works because it adds fold equity that
  a preflop shove denies — many opponents will fold a hand that would
  have called preflop when faced with an all-in on the flop, especially
  if they missed; correct use requires the opponent to be a cbetting
  player who can also fold; against calling stations the preflop shove
  is almost always better because the fold equity advantage disappears

Typical drills: "You are second in chips at the bubble. A medium stack shoves
all-in. You have 77. Your cEV says call, but what does ICM say? Why?"

**W27: Database And Stats**

Cognitive shift: over a large sample, statistics tell the truth about
patterns that are invisible in a session. Database review is a professional
discipline, not optional homework.

Core concepts:
- HUD stats: VPIP, PFR, AF, 3-bet%, fold-to-3-bet%, WTSD, WSD —
  and fold-to-CBet (F-CB): the percentage of time a player folds when
  facing a continuation bet; one of the most actionable stats in low-to-mid
  stakes play; opponents with F-CB above 55–60% are auto-profitable CBet targets;
  opponents with F-CB below 35% should rarely be bluffed postflop;
  F-CB is more directly useful for daily in-game decisions than fold-to-3-bet%
  because CBet situations arise every hand; the correct threshold varies by
  position, street (flop F-CB vs turn F-CB), and board texture
- WWSF (Won $ When Saw Flop): the percentage of pots you win when you see a
  flop — includes winning without showdown (opponent folds) and at showdown;
  a standard healthy value is 47–52%; below 45% indicates either too much
  passive check-folding postflop or playing too many flops with weak hands;
  above 56% may indicate over-aggression on weak equity spots that works
  short-term but is unsustainable; WWSF is the primary diagnostic for
  postflop aggression and initiative — where WTSD/WSD diagnose showdown
  habits, WWSF diagnoses how often you are willing to fight for pots before
  showdown; combining red-line (non-showdown) trend with WWSF gives a
  complete picture of postflop aggression profile
- Red line vs blue line (non-showdown winnings): database software graphs two
  separate winnings curves — the red line tracks non-showdown profit/loss
  (hands where no showdown occurs — folds to your bets or you fold); the blue
  line tracks showdown profit/loss; a player with a steeply falling red line
  is bluffing too infrequently or giving up too often; a player with a poor
  blue line is calling too loose at showdown; the combined total is the
  overall winrate; professional diagnostic work starts by identifying which
  line is the primary drag — fixing a red-line leak (more aggression and
  fewer give-ups) is fundamentally different from fixing a blue-line leak
  (tighter calling ranges at showdown)
- Reading population stats: what deviates from expected ranges at your stake?
- Leak finding: which spots lose you the most over large samples?
- Hand history filters: isolating specific situations (BTN open, 3-bet pots)
- Database software basics: PokerTracker / Hold'em Manager workflow
- Statistical sample sizes: when is a read reliable? (PFR needs 50+ hands)

Typical drills: "Your WTSD% is 34% and WSD% is 46%. Is this a leak? What
does it suggest about your river calling range?"

**W28: Bankroll Management And Game Selection**

Cognitive shift: a technically skilled player can go broke by playing the
wrong stakes or the wrong games. Bankroll management and game selection are
professional disciplines that protect skill from variance.

Core concepts:
- Bankroll requirements: recommended buy-in minimums by format (cash vs MTT)
- Moving up in stakes: objective criteria, not confidence or impatience
- Moving down in stakes: ego barrier, variance protection, the correct trigger
- Stop-loss rules: session-level and monthly loss limits as bankroll protection
- Hourly EV: how to estimate expected value per hour at a given game
- Win rate realism: a winning cash game player earns 3–10bb/100 hands at
  their peak stake; a solid MTT player achieves 20–50% ROI in soft fields;
  these numbers feel small to beginners who expect to win far more per session;
  at 5bb/100 and 100bb buy-ins, a 10,000-hand downswing of 150–200bb is
  statistically normal — the math of poker variance is genuinely unintuitive
  until confronted explicitly; understanding expected win rates is the most
  effective inoculation against stake-selection errors and premature move-ups
- Rake and its impact on optimal strategy: rake is a cost deducted from every
  pot — at micro stakes rake can be 5-10% of the pot and changes optimal play
  significantly; opening ranges should tighten under high rake, thin value bets
  become less profitable, marginal draws become less worth chasing;
  rakeback and VIP programs are part of the real hourly rate calculation;
  the player who ignores rake at low stakes is systematically making sub-optimal
  decisions without knowing why
- Table quality metrics: VPIP distribution at the table, fish count, stack sizes
- Seat selection: position relative to aggressive and passive players;
  the highest-value seat at any table is directly to the LEFT of the most
  recreational or weakest player — sitting left of whale means you have
  position on them for every hand they play and can isolate them IP;
  sitting to their right means you are OOP against the one player you most
  want to play against; at a live table, moving seats to gain position on
  a recreational player is a legal, standard professional move and among
  the highest-EV non-strategic actions available at a live game; in online
  play, choosing an open seat to the left of a known fish at table selection
  is equally valuable
- Site selection: which networks and time zones have the softest games?
- Rakeback and bonuses: how rake impacts hourly at different stakes
- Short-stack cash game strategy (intentional short buy-in): some players
  deliberately buy in for 40bb or less; this strategy reduces variance,
  simplifies postflop decisions (most hands become shove-or-fold), and
  can be profitable against weaker opponents who are not adjusted to
  playing vs short stacks; the tradeoffs: implied odds are severely
  reduced for speculative hands, opponents tighten up against short stacks,
  and you cannot extract deep-stack value from big hands; short-stack cash
  strategy is not simply "play the same but with fewer chips" — it requires
  understanding that the value of position and playability is compressed
  and the value of equity when all-in is amplified
- Risk of ruin: the mathematical probability of losing an entire bankroll
  before running good; at 20 buy-ins, the risk of ruin for a 5bb/100 winner
  is meaningful; at 50 buy-ins, it approaches zero for any realistic win rate;
  risk of ruin is the foundational reason bankroll requirements exist —
  it is not pessimism but math; a player who dismisses bankroll management
  as unnecessary is implicitly accepting a non-trivial probability of going
  broke even if they are winning players; the 20/50/100 buy-in recommendations
  for cash/MTT/SNG are the points where risk of ruin falls below a threshold
  that professional players consider acceptable
- Shot-taking: a deliberate, bounded move to a higher stake to test competitiveness
  at that level; the professional discipline is: designate a fixed number of buy-ins
  for the shot (typically 5–10), play at the higher stake until you either win
  a specific threshold (e.g., double the shot roll) or lose it all, then return
  to the lower stake if the shot fails; shot-taking differs from moving up because
  the roll is ring-fenced — a failed shot does not endanger the primary bankroll;
  premature shots without ring-fencing are the most common way players lose their
  bankroll to variance rather than to actual competitive inferiority
- Life roll separation: poker bankroll must be kept entirely separate from
  living expenses, emergency funds, and savings; playing with money earmarked
  for rent or bills creates pressure that degrades decision quality and makes
  mathematically correct folds emotionally difficult; the practical test:
  if losing your entire current poker bankroll tonight would change your
  life outside poker, it is not a true poker bankroll — it is life money;
  life roll separation is the most important structural discipline for any
  player who treats poker as income
- No-flop-no-drop: a standard rake rule at many live casinos and some online
  rooms where rake is only collected if a flop is dealt; this means preflop
  all-ins, folds to the open, and raises that win the pot preflop are rake-free;
  strategically, this changes the math on blind steals, 3-bets that end preflop,
  and squeeze plays — they capture the pot without paying rake; knowing this
  rule at a live table means preflop aggression has a higher real EV than the
  raw pot-odds calculation suggests, and that open-shoving short stacks in
  tournaments where no-flop-no-drop applies is even more profitable than the
  chip-EV calculation alone shows

Typical drills: "You have 30 buy-ins for NL50 and run bad, dropping to 22.
Do you move down? What is your trigger? Two tables are available at your
stake: one with two regulars and two fish; one with all regulars. Which
do you choose and why?"

**W29: Study Methodology**

Cognitive shift: the best players do not just study more — they study
systematically. The study loop (play, tag, review, fix, test) is a
professional discipline that compounds over time.

Core concepts:
- The study loop: tagging hands during play → reviewing in solver → building a fix
- Solver workflow: setting up a realistic spot, reading output, implementing takeaway
- Hand review with peers: how to give and receive feedback productively
- Leak prioritization: fix the highest-frequency leak first, not the most
  interesting one
- Database keeping: session notes, stake history, results tracking
- Avoiding study traps: watching training videos without active problem-solving
  does not improve results
- GTO trainer tools: platforms like GTO Wizard, Vision GTO, and similar
  trainers allow drilling specific spots without building solver trees;
  the trainer generates the solver solution, you make a real-time decision,
  and receive immediate feedback and EV loss data; this active drill loop
  builds intuition faster than passive video watching; GTO trainer work
  should become the primary daily study tool after W22 is complete
- Multi-tabling strategy: adding tables increases volume but decreases
  decision quality; optimal table count depends on how thoroughly current
  ranges are internalized; start at 2 tables and expand only when both can
  be played with zero autopilot hands; winrate per 100 hands is a more
  honest metric than raw hourly volume; multi-tabling should be a deliberate
  scaling decision at a specific stage of improvement, not the default
  starting point for a new online player
- The four stages of competence applied to poker: unconscious incompetence
  ("I don't know what I don't know" — the new player who doesn't see leaks);
  conscious incompetence ("I know I make mistakes but can't yet fix them" —
  the student who sees the problem); conscious competence ("I can play
  correctly but must think deliberately" — the improving regular); unconscious
  competence ("correct decisions happen automatically" — the professional);
  each stage requires a different study focus: identifying gaps at stage 1,
  building correction reps at stage 2, drilling to automation at stage 3,
  and then studying novel spots to push the frontier at stage 4;
  knowing which stage you are in for each skill cluster prevents the common
  mistake of studying advanced content before fundamentals are automated

Typical drills: "You lost 3 buy-ins in 3-bet pots as the preflop caller last
month. Write the first three steps of your study plan to address this leak."

**W30: Live Poker Mastery**

Cognitive shift: live poker is a different environment, not just online poker
without a HUD. Physical presence, tells, timing, table dynamics, and casino
etiquette create a layer of information and pressure that does not exist online.
The learner who masters live play has a genuine edge most online-trained players
never develop.

Core concepts:
- Physical tells: genuine vs. performed strength/weakness signals
  (shaking hands, chip handling, eye contact, sudden stillness)
- Timing tells: tanking with strong hands, snap-acting with weak or very strong
- Speech play: what players say at the table and how to interpret it
- Bet-sizing tells live: players often subconsciously size differently
  with strong vs. bluff hands when using physical chips
- Avoiding giving off tells: routines, consistent timing, neutral posture
- Live game pace: fewer hands per hour — patience and selectivity matter more
- Casino etiquette: angle-shooting, string bets, acting out of turn, muck rules
- HUD-less adjustment: reading players without stats — building a session-level
  mental model of each opponent's tendencies
- Live bankroll: travel, hotel, variance buffer for live sessions
  (live variance is higher per hour than online due to fewer hands)
- Live vs. online strategy divergence: which adjustments are most profitable
- Ante games and straddle: live cash games frequently feature mandatory or
  voluntary straddles — an extra blind posted by UTG or another position;
  a straddle enlarges the pot, shifts who acts last preflop, and increases
  effective SPR pressure; knowing when to straddle, when to avoid it, and
  how to adjust opening and calling ranges with a straddle live is a
  distinct skill from standard cash game play
- Bomb pots: a live cash game format where every player posts an agreed ante
  and the hand goes directly to the flop with no preflop betting; this
  creates very large pots, multiway fields with completely uncapped ranges,
  and high-SPR postflop decisions that require a distinct strategy — wider
  bet-into-the-field value ranges and more conservative bluffing in multiway
  bomb pot pots vs heads-up situations
- Run it twice (RIT): an option in many live cash games (and some online)
  to deal the remaining streets twice when two players are all-in; the EV
  of the hand is identical to running it once — RIT does not change expected
  value, only variance; the correct decision is based on bankroll context:
  a player with a small bankroll relative to the pot should prefer RIT
  to reduce variance; a player who has a large edge in the game may
  rationally decline RIT when they believe running it once gives them
  more information-gathering opportunities; understanding that RIT is a
  pure variance-reduction mechanism (not an EV play) prevents common
  misconceptions about its strategic value
- Live casino procedural rules: verbal action is binding — saying "raise" at
  a live table commits you to a raise even before you touch chips; a string
  bet (making a raise by putting out chips in multiple motions without
  declaring "raise" first) is illegal and will be called as a call only;
  the bet line is the physical line at most live tables — chips pushed past
  it are committed, chips behind it are not; protecting your hand means
  placing a card cap or chip on your hole cards so the dealer does not
  accidentally muck them; acting out of turn gives opponents information
  and is considered poor etiquette — and in formal games, the floor may
  enforce a binding action on the offending player; these procedural rules
  are not strategic but a live player who is unaware of them will make
  binding mistakes that cost money regardless of their poker skill

Typical drills:
- "Villain tanks for 45 seconds, then shoves the river. Is this more likely
  strength or weakness? What behavioral patterns support each?"
- "You notice a player always looks at their chips when they are about to bet
  before the action reaches them. How do you use this?"
- "You have been at a live table for 3 hours. You have no stats. What is your
  mental model of each opponent based only on observed hands?"

What the learner can do after W30:
- Sit at a live casino table and read the environment, not just the cards
- Maintain a neutral tell-free routine under pressure
- Build a session-level player map without any software
- Recognize and name the five most common physical tells with reasonable accuracy

### Block 7 — Elite (W31–W36)
#### Ceiling: super-strong / elite player

**W31: Mixed Strategy Internalization**

Cognitive shift: at the highest level, being predictable is exploitable even
against average opponents. The learner must be able to mix strategies
intuitively — not by memorizing frequencies, but by understanding when
indifference is required.

Core concepts:
- When must you mix? When an opponent can profitably deviate if you play pure
- Practical mixing without a solver: using table clock, bet sizing patterns,
  or position as randomization devices
- Indifference revisited: building a feel for threshold equity spots
- GTO at live tables: how much of solver output is usable without a HUD?
- Mixed strategies in bluffing: not every session should look the same
- Constructing naturally mixed lines: using bet sizing to induce different
  responses rather than randomizing mechanically

Typical drills: "You should bluff the river 35% of the time in this spot.
You have no randomization device. How do you practically implement this
at a live table?"

**W32: Node-Lock And Advanced Exploit**

Cognitive shift: a true exploitative player does not just deviate from GTO —
they model the opponent's deviation and solve the best counter-strategy
against that specific leak.

Core concepts:
- Node-locking in solvers: fixing an opponent's strategy to a specific
  tendency and re-solving your counter-strategy
- Over-folding counter-strategies: how to maximally exploit a folding opponent
- Over-calling counter-strategies: how to construct a range that beats a
  calling station
- The exploit-protection trade-off: how much EV do you give up by staying
  balanced vs going fully exploitative?
- Dynamic exploitation: adjusting within a session as opponent adjusts back
- Population vs individual exploits: using stats vs using session observations
- Deep stack play (200bb+): at very deep effective stacks, the strategic
  landscape changes fundamentally — implied odds on speculative hands increase
  dramatically (set-mining and suited connectors become significantly more
  profitable), 3-bet ranges widen because there is more room to maneuver
  post-flop, pot control with strong hands becomes more important, and
  multi-street implied odds planning replaces SPR-commitment heuristics
  as the primary decision framework; a player excellent at 100bb who has
  never studied 200bb play is playing a different game without knowing it;
  deep stack play particularly appears in live poker, high-stakes cash,
  and the early levels of large MTTs

Typical drills: "Villain folds to flop CBets 72% of the time (population is
55%). Node-lock their fold frequency. What does the solver now recommend
for your CBet frequency and sizing?"

**W33: Heads-Up Mastery**

Cognitive shift: heads-up is not a harder version of 6-max — it is a
fundamentally different game. Every hand is contested. There are no other
players to fold. Position alternates every hand. Aggression and rapid adaptation
are the primary currencies. A player who cannot hold their own heads-up has a
critical gap in the complete skill set.

Core concepts:
- HU preflop ranges: dramatically wider than 6-max; almost any two cards has
  strategic value when position is the primary filter
- Positional dominance: BTN/SB opens close to 100% and defends wide;
  BB defends an extremely wide range to prevent automatic stealing
- Aggression imperative: checking in HU spots surrenders equity and information;
  constant pressure is the default, not a style choice
- Nut advantage in HU: both players have wide, flat ranges — nut advantage
  on specific board textures becomes the primary betting justification
- Adaptation speed: HU adjustments must happen within 20–30 hands, not over a
  session; the opponent who adapts faster wins the meta-game
- Limping strategy: HU is the only format where BTN limping is a legitimate
  equilibrium tool — limping protects a range and denies isolation
- Leveling intensity: third and fourth-level thinking is most active heads-up;
  "what does villain think I think they have?" matters more than anywhere else
- Psychological pressure: in live HU, posture, timing, and table presence
  carry real strategic weight — knowing how to apply and absorb pressure
- Online HU tools: with only one opponent, HUD stats reach useful sample sizes
  faster; VPIP, AFq, and fold-to-3bet become actionable within one session

Typical drills:
- "You are HU on the BTN/SB. Villain folds to 3-bets 68% of the time. How does
  your 3-bet range change vs a 6-max game where villain folds 45%?"
- "After 25 hands, villain starts check-raising your CBets on paired boards.
  What adjustment do you make and why?"
- "You have Q7o on the BTN heads-up. Open, limp, or raise? Walk through the
  argument and why Q7o is not a fold in this context."

What the learner can do after W33:
- Play a HU match without feeling strategically lost or reverting to
  passive 6-max habits
- Identify the three most common exploitable HU patterns within a session
- Adjust aggression, limping frequency, and 3-bet ranges within a single HU match
- Understand why HU is the purest test of all technical skills combined

**W34: Mental Game At Professional Level**

Cognitive shift: mental game is not soft. It is a technical discipline with
identifiable problems and systematic fixes. At this level, mental leaks
directly cost buy-ins per session.

Core concepts:
- Tilt taxonomy: injustice tilt, desperation tilt, fear of loss, entitlement tilt,
  winner's tilt; winner's tilt is the least-discussed but highly costly pattern
  where a player who is running well or winning big in a session begins playing
  too loose, too speculatively, or too hero-call-heavy in an attempt to keep
  the winning streak alive or maximize the session result; it manifests as
  wider opens in spots that should be folds, calling down marginal rivers
  "because I'm up so it's fine," and stacking off with hands that should be
  folded; recognizing winner's tilt requires noticing when session profits are
  changing decision quality — the correct intervention is the same as any
  tilt: name it, return to process, consider stopping
- The performance state: how to recognize when you are playing A-game vs B-game
- Variance psychology at high stakes: how sample size and expected swings change
  emotional load
- Recovery protocols: between-session and in-session emotional reset tools
- Career variance: downswings that last months, not sessions
- Long-term perspective: separating skill development from results trajectory

Typical drills: "You have lost 30 buy-ins over the last month at a +5bb/100
stake. Walk through the mental and technical checklist before deciding
whether to move down."

**W35: Peak Performance**

Cognitive shift: professional consistency is not natural — it is engineered.
Session preparation, physical and cognitive energy management, and sustainable
career structure determine the ceiling of long-run performance.

Core concepts:
- Pre-session routine: warm-up review, goal-setting, mental state check
- Energy management: sleep, exercise, nutrition, and their direct impact on
  decision quality
- Volume targets: how to set sustainable weekly volume goals without burnout
- Stopping rules revisited: session-ending rules that protect both bankroll
  and decision quality
- Career structure: coach relationships, peer groups, continuous study budget
- Long-game perspective: skill development vs stake maximization as competing
  goals at different career stages
- Staking and selling action: a professional-level funding mechanism where
  an investor (backer) provides bankroll in exchange for a share of profits;
  the standard backing deal structure is a profit split (e.g., 50/50 or 60/40)
  with makeup — the player must repay any losses before receiving their profit
  share; markup refers to selling a percentage of your tournament action above
  face value (e.g., selling 10% of a $1,000 buy-in for $120 instead of $100)
  based on your demonstrated edge; selling action at markup above 1.3x without
  a documented win rate is generally considered poor practice in the community;
  the complete player understands the financial mechanics of backing, knows how
  to evaluate a deal, and can calculate their real hourly expectation under
  a makeup structure — because taking a bad staking deal can make a winning
  player economically negative even on a profitable run

Typical drills: "Design a one-week preparation plan for an important live
tournament. Include study, physical prep, review schedule, and in-session
focus anchors."

**W36: The Complete Player**

Cognitive shift: at this level there is no single skill to add — there is only
the integration of everything. The complete player is not the one who studied
the most theory, but the one who can deploy the right tool from the right block
at the right moment: variance mindset when under pressure, exploitative reads
when the opponent telegraphs, GTO discipline when the opponent is unknown,
live tells when the table allows it, ICM precision when the money matters.

Core concepts:
- Integration audit: identifying which blocks in the curriculum have become
  automatic vs. which still require conscious effort
- The complete player profile: technical (solver, ranges, blockers) + adaptive
  (reads, exploits, live) + mental (variance, tilt, peak performance)
- Skill plateau recognition: how to diagnose why improvement has stalled
- Long-term study architecture: coach, peer group, solver, database, volume
- Legacy and teaching: explaining your own thinking to others as the deepest
  test of genuine understanding
- Career positioning: cash, MTT, live, mixed — knowing which format suits
  your strengths and life structure
- Other game formats awareness: NLHE is one variant in a larger ecosystem;
  Pot Limit Omaha (PLO) uses 4 hole cards and requires exactly 2 — this
  single rule change makes hand-reading fundamentally different and nut hands
  far more common; Short Deck (6+ Hold'em) removes cards 2–5, altering
  equity distributions and standard hand rankings; Omaha Hi/Lo splits the
  pot between the best high hand and the best low hand; Spin & Go / Jackpot
  SNGs are hyper-fast 3-player tournaments with a random prize multiplier;
  the complete player knows these formats exist, can describe them accurately,
  and has made a deliberate choice about which to pursue — being surprised by
  a PLO table invitation is a knowledge gap, not a variance problem
- Sit-and-Go (SNG) format: a tournament with a fixed number of seats (typically
  6, 9, or 18 players) that begins as soon as all seats are filled — no
  scheduled start time; blind levels increase on a clock, not a schedule;
  SNGs are shorter than MTTs and require faster stack-depth adaptation; the
  strategy resembles late-stage MTT play for the entire event because the
  field is small from the start; bubble play in a 9-player SNG (pay top 3)
  begins at 4 players remaining — a large proportion of the total event;
  SNGs reward ICM awareness earlier than MTTs and punish stack-off mistakes
  more severely per unit of time; the hyper-turbo SNG (HUSNG, 2-player
  hyper-turbo) is the most mathematically refined poker format and has
  been solved to near-equilibrium at many stack depths

Typical drills:
- "Rate your own competency in each of the 7 curriculum blocks. Where is your
  biggest gap? What does the next 6 months of study look like?"
- "Describe your last losing session using only process language — no outcome
  words. What did the variance mindset actually feel like in the moment?"
- "Teach a beginner the concept of range advantage in under 3 minutes.
  If you can teach it simply, you own it."

After W36: volume accumulation, solver depth work, and peer-level review are
the only remaining levers. No structured course closes the gap between W36
and the top of the game — that is the territory of lifelong professional
self-improvement.
