## Why A World Must Be More Than A Tiny Handful Of Nodes

A world that has only a couple of nodes can look complete on a map while still failing as curriculum.

That creates three risks:

- shallow first-pass understanding
- weak repetition and transfer
- no room for review, variation, or later mastery reinforcement

Practical implication:

- a world should not be judged only by whether it has a named entry point
- it should be judged by whether it has enough structured volume to carry its intended cognitive job

## Practical Structure Of A Healthy World

Exact counts do not need to be final yet, but a healthy world should usually contain:

- more than one major node family or sub-cluster
- enough micro-sessions or campaign slices to move beyond one exposed concept
- at least one repetition path and one review path
- at least one apply-oriented layer, not only intro recognition

Typical internal shape:

1. entry / orientation node or micro-cluster
2. focused practice cluster
3. apply / decision cluster
4. recap / review / resurfacing layer

This can be implemented through campaign nodes, session-drill clusters, or both.
The key is that the world has enough volume to support the rhythm, not just the label.

## First-Pass Depth vs Long-Term Retention

First-pass depth means:

- the learner can encounter the skill in a clean introductory form
- the learner gets enough repetitions to build a stable first model
- the learner sees at least one slightly different application of the same idea

Long-term retention capacity means:

- the world can resurface the skill later
- review or recap can revisit the idea without rebuilding the world
- the skill can connect forward into later worlds without becoming one forgotten micro-slice

Practical rule:

- a world can be minimally viable without full long-term retention machinery
- a scalable world should be designed to support later resurfacing and reapplication

## Minimum Rhythm Requirement Inside A World

Every healthy world should have a visible rhythm:

- intro
- practice
- apply
- review

These do not always need separate screens.
They can be distributed across nodes or clustered inside a few strong nodes.
But all four jobs must exist somewhere inside the world’s structure.

Interpretation:

- `intro` establishes the concept cleanly
- `practice` builds pattern fluency
- `apply` makes the learner use the skill in a slightly more decision-like form
- `review` prevents the world from becoming a one-pass dead end

## Judging World Density

### Too Thin

A world is too thin when most of the following are true:

- it has only one meaningful node family
- it introduces a skill but does not repeat it enough to stabilize
- it has no clear apply layer
- it has no review or recap capacity
- it depends on later worlds to do most of its own teaching job

### Minimally Viable

A world is minimally viable when:

- its intended skill family is clearly represented
- it has at least a basic intro/practice/apply shape
- it has some bounded recap or review support
- it can function as a real bridge to the next world without a major conceptual hole

### Healthy / Scalable

A world is healthy or scalable when:

- it has multiple node families or strong sub-clusters
- repetition, application, and review are all present in a reusable way
- its main skill family does not depend on one narrow pilot slice
- later mastery, resurfacing, or cross-world transfer can build on it cleanly

## Node-Family Density Expectations

Practical guidance:

- a major world should usually have more than one node-family bucket over time
- one live pilot node or one session cluster can start a world bridge
- that does not by itself mean the world has healthy density yet

Examples from current canon:

- World 1 is moving toward healthy density because it has Act0, spine, followups, and cross-world review participation
- World 2 currently has a valid first bridge slice, but is still not a dense world
- coarse scaffold worlds are structurally present but should still be treated as density gaps until more than a shell exists

## Skeleton Existence vs Meaningful Density vs Retention Loop

Three separate states should stay explicit:

### Skeleton Exists

- the world appears in topology or planning
- there is at least a structural home for future content

### Meaningful Density Exists

- enough world volume exists to teach the intended core job in a believable way

### Retention Loop Exists

- review, recap, resurfacing, or later reapplication paths exist so the world does not behave like disposable content

Practical rule:

- do not confuse scaffold existence with a healthy world
- do not confuse one pilot slice with full density
- do not confuse first-pass density with a real retention loop

## How This Canon Should Guide Later Work

This canon should later inform:

- skeleton expansion decisions
- fill prioritization
- readiness judgments
- future density guards, if the current planning stack proves stable enough

Near-term decision rule:

1. ask whether the world has a real intro/practice/apply/review rhythm
2. ask whether the world has enough node-family depth to support its intended skill job
3. ask whether current implementation is only a pilot bridge or a dense world layer
4. upgrade world status or readiness only when density is real, not merely when something exists

## Near-Term Implication

Near-term rollout should treat:

- World 1 as the current healthiest density model
- World 2 as a valid bridge world in progress, not yet a healthy dense world
- coarse Worlds 3-10 as structural shells that still need real density planning and fill

This prevents the ladder from drifting into a shallow shape where every world exists on paper but too few worlds carry enough volume to teach and retain their intended skills.
