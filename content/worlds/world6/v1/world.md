# World 6

## Overview
- World 6 is the learner-facing home for scaffolded range thinking.
- This world shifts the learner from single-hand decisions toward bucket-based reasoning:
  what stronger and weaker hands can exist,
  how wide or narrow a range feels,
  and how that shape changes by street.
- The goal is not solver-grade precision. The goal is to make range ideas concrete enough that learners can compare broad hand families, notice range advantage, and follow how ranges compress or polarize as the hand develops.
- This world comes after board texture, draws, and improvement counting. Once learners can read what the board is doing, the next step is understanding that one visible hand is part of a wider distribution of likely hands.

## Goals
- Teach range buckets in plain language rather than abstract combinatorics.
- Introduce range width as a practical idea:
  some positions and lines contain more hands,
  some contain fewer,
  and that changes who can pressure profitably.
- Show how flop and turn cards change range shape through advantage, compression, and narrowing.
- Introduce river polarization as a bounded learner-facing concept:
  ranges can end up weighted toward stronger made hands or clearer bluffs.
- Prepare learners for later worlds where stack depth, tournament pressure, and exploit logic change how range-based decisions should be applied.

## Completion criteria
- The world clearly owns scaffolded range thinking rather than single-hand-only reasoning.
- Learners can describe the difference between a wider and narrower range in plain language.
- Learners can recognize early range advantage, turn compression, and basic river polarization without needing solver output.
- World 6 remains bounded:
  no dense combinatorics,
  no solver-mimic precision,
  no specialization-owned format policy.
