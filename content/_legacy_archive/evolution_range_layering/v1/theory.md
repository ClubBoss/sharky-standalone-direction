# What it is
Range layering is the practice of distributing your combos across distinct roles—value, bluff, protection, merge, thin value, and block. Rather than thinking only about which hands live in a range, layering describes how you weight each combo so it contributes to aggression, defense, or coverage without leaking equity.

# Why it matters
A layered range keeps EV stable by ensuring aggression is supported, protection hands guard the bottom end, and bluffs carry the right blockers. It prevents over-bluffing, stops range collapse when opponents pressure, and gives you flexible leverage to match the table’s balance while maintaining consistent coverage.

# Rules of thumb
- Build the value layer first with top combos you are happy to play for full size; these anchor the range.
- Source bluffs from the weakest equity hands that hold blockers to the opponent’s key holdings.
- Protection hands are medium-equity combos that can deny realization when you check or call without turning into dead money.
- Merge hands straddle value and protection, allowing you to switch between aggression and defense depending on how the action unfolds.
- Guard the value band from thinning too much; if you replace strong combos with weaker ones you lose the baseline EV.
- Keep the bluff band uncluttered; extra medium-strength hands make bluffs easy to counter.
- Think thin value as the bridge that picks up medium equity while threatening fold equity and preventing your bluff weight from dominating a single area.

# Mini example
You start with twelve big-bet combos: assign four to pure value, four to bluffs (all holding showdown blockers), two to protection calls that survive raise-heavy lines, and two merge combos that can either bet or check depending on opponent aggression. This layered structure ensures the top of range stays threatening while the rest of the range fulfills strategic roles.

# Common mistakes
- Over-bluffing with hands that lack blockers; they fold out too little and bleed EV when called.
- Using medium-strength combos as bluffs, leaving nothing for the merge or protection layers.
- Missing merge spots where one hand could shift between value and protection without signaling intent.
- Failing to honor a protection layer, so marginal hands get run over by aggression.
- Overprotecting marginal holdings and leaving no room for bluffs or merge combos.
- Flattening the range into a single linear band and forgetting to segment roles.

# Workflow
- Identify the value band you need in this spot and weight those combos heavily.
- Mark the weakest blockers and nominate them as bluff candidates.
- Layer in protection hands that allow you to defend against aggression without folding.
- Scan for merge opportunities where a hand can toggle between aggression and defense.
- Verify blockers and density so each layer still covers opponent ranges.
- Sanity-check frequencies: ensure bluffs, protections, and merges execute without overwhelming one another.

# Mini-glossary
- value : combos you are comfortable playing for full pot and sizing them up.
- bluff : zero-to-low equity combos used to deny opponent equity when your blockers line up.
- protection : hands that absorb pressure without folding, shielding the range’s tail.
- merge : hands that sit between value and protection and flex between the two.
- thin value : medium-equity hands that still add EV by betting for value when fold equity exists.
- block bet : a small bet that cancels opponent aggression while charging draws.
- coverage : the idea that your range handles every major texture or action without holes.
- density : how many combos you allocate to each strategic segment.
- blockers : cards that remove combos from your opponent’s range, improving bluff equity.
- removal : reducing opponent combinations to shift their equity distribution.
- equity realization : the proportion of theoretical equity a hand can actually convert based on playability.

# EV
Layered ranges allow you to attack with confidence, protect vulnerable slots, and reduce opponent exploitation. When each combo fulfills a clear role, your aggression stays balanced, protection works without waste, and the long-run EV reflects disciplined frequency rather than reactive guesswork.
