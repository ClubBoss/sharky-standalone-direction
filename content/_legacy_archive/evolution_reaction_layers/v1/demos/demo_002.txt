# Scenario
Attacker escalates pressure; defender must deduce whether the reaction layer stays fold-heavy, shifts to call-heavy, or goes raise-heavy.

# Snapshot
Attacker: stronger top/mid continuity, layered aggression. Defender: visible caps, mid-band fragility, hesitant to transition to raises. Pressure cue: climbing aggression with blockers.

# Player task
Name the dominant reaction layer (fold-heavy, call-heavy, or raise-heavy) and justify using pressure, caps, and density cues.

# Expected reasoning
Stronger continuity keeps pressure rising, so defender caps tighten and prefer defensive responses. Mid fragility means they cannot raise without collapsing, yet they rarely fold because caps offer some resistance. This combination points to fold-heavy reaction: they fold under peak pressure but still cling to capped mid combos before capitulating. Raise-heavy fails because caps prevent aggression; call-heavy fails because they fold when overpressed.

# Correct line
Fold-heavy reaction layer: rising pressure collapses the fragile caps, leaving mostly folds.

# Why not the alternatives
- Call-heavy: defenders do not keep calling under the peak pressure.
- Raise-heavy: they cannot raise when caps tighten.
- Passive: ignores the pressure spike that forces reactions.
- Timing: misreads fold resistances as calls.
- Mixed: overcomplicates a clear fold-heavy tilt.
