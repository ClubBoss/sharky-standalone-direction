# What it is
Reaction layers describe how your range responds to pressure tiers, advantage shifts, and density changes. They capture top-end contraction, mid-band shifts, and fragility collapses so you understand which combos tighten, which add defenders, and where the range breaks down under stress.

# Why it matters
Mapping reaction layers stabilizes EV in the presence of aggression by preventing overfolding, spotting counter-pressure nodes, and protecting mid-band equity before it collapses. When pressure echoes move through the tree, a clear reaction plan lets you absorb force without leaking value or handing leverage back to the opponent.

- Strong ranges contract their top-end slowly, keeping power available while still applying pressure.
- Fragile ranges collapse in the mid-band first, so protect those combos before the opponent overleans.
- Counter-pressure requires both blockers and density, not just pure aggression.
- Passive pools widen protection layers, so react with additional calling combos.
- Over-aggressive pools induce stabilizer reactions that favor tight, resilient hands.
- Reaction layers mirror pressure tiers, not raw equity, so align responses accordingly.

# Mini example
A high-pressure range pushes its aggressive combos, while the opponent shrinks its mid-band into a catch mode and trims weak bluffs, creating a stabilizer shell that resists pressure until the attacker overextends.

# Common mistakes
- Confusing reaction layers with advantage and treating them like static combos.
- Over-expanding bluff weight during a collapse, which gives the opponent a clear calling target.
- Under-protecting fragile regions, leaving the middle of the range exposed to pressure.
- Forcing counter-pressure without blockers, turning aggression into bluff noise.
- Assuming reactions stay symmetrical across spots instead of varying with pressure echoes.
- Ignoring stabilizer zones that should defend rather than fight.

# Workflow
- Assess incoming pressure to understand how your range is being pushed.
- Identify collapse zones where mid-bank or weak combos are rattled.
- Classify stabilizer layers that must survive until the pressure breaks.
- Map counter-pressure potential and determine where retaliation is viable.
- Adjust bluff and value bands to reinforce the reaction layers.
- Verify the equilibrium or exploit shift before locking in the line.

# Mini-glossary
- reaction layer : the part of your range that flexes under pressure.
- collapse zone : the area where your distribution gives way and folds out.
- stabilizer : combos that defend key lines and absorb aggression.
- counter-pressure : the retaliation pressure you can apply back.
- density shift : when opponent ranges add or remove combos in an area.
- mid-band : the middle segment of the range between top value and bluffs.
- fragility : how easily a zone collapses under aggression.
- top-end contraction : tightening the strongest combos to preserve leverage.
- adjustment path : the sequence of tweaks you make in response to pressure.
- pressure echo : the repeated effect of pressure reverberating through the tree.

# EV
When you respect reaction layers, EV leakage shrinks because adjustments follow pressure geometry instead of guesswork. Solid reactions keep bluffs credible, preserve mid-band equity, and reclaim initiative whenever the opponent overreaches, turning pressure into predictable profit.
