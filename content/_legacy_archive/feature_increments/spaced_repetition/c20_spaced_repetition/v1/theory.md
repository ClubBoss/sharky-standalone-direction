### C20 Spaced Repetition: Retention for Poker Decisions

Spaced repetition turns study into durable habits by resurfacing concepts right before you forget them. Poker needs this because spots are varied: SRP vs 3BP, IP vs OOP, dry vs dynamic boards, and ICM pressure all blur together without structured review. Spacing locks heuristics like small cbet on dry boards or check_back on paired textures into long-term memory so they show up automatically in live play.

The schedule matters. Early reviews are close together: 1 hour after first exposure, then 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, and 14 days. Each window pushes recall just as memory fades, strengthening retrieval. After two clean passes at 14 days, you can stretch to 21 or 30 days. Missed items reset to an earlier window, often back to 1 day. This predictable ladder keeps bandwidth low while keeping skills active.

Micro-quizzes and drills are the fuel. Each micro question ties to a single rule: range edge on dry boards means cbet_small; paired boards reduce bluff success so check_back; undersized cbet in ICM is capped so raise or float; overbluff nodes invite bluff-catch calls with blockers. Drills add sequence: flop plan, turn adjustment, river decision. Feeding results into a scheduler ensures weak spots return sooner while strong spots move forward.

Tagging misses is critical. When you fail a prompt, attach a tag: position (UTG, MP, CO, BTN, SB, BB), pot type (SRP, 3BP), board class (dry, dynamic, paired), and leak type (overfold, overcall, overbluff, undersized cbet). The scheduler uses these tags to reshuffle similar items near-term, so patterns you struggle with repeat before they slip. Correct answers move forward; wrong answers drop back without punishment, just more reps.

Spacing differs live versus online. Live sessions have fewer hands per hour, so review between sessions should be tighter: 1 hour post-session, then 1 day, 3 days, 7 days. Online volume lets you test sooner; insert an in-session micro block every 90 minutes, then keep the same 1 day, 3 day, 7 day cadence. Near events with high ICM pressure, add a short focused block on bubble and payjump spots the day before play.

Implementation workflow: pick a batch of micro-quizzes and drills, run them, log misses with tags, then schedule. Day 0: learn and quiz. After 1 hour: quick review of misses. Day 1: repeat all misses plus a handful of new items. Day 3 and 7: mix old and new, retire items you consistently pass. Day 14: confirm long-term retention; extend to day 21 if clean. Keep sessions short (10-15 minutes) to avoid fatigue and maintain focus.

Contrast with cramming: mass review feels productive but decays fast. Contrast with GTO study: balance alone does not secure recall without repetition. Spaced repetition intentionally delays recall to strengthen memory, prioritizes weak items, and keeps total time low. For poker, that means fast recognition of range edge, nut edge, blocker relevance, and fold equity across SRP and 3BP, IP and OOP, without clogging mental bandwidth. Stick to the ladder, tag misses, and let the scheduler pull the right prompt at the right moment.
