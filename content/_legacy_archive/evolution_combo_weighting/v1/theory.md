# What it is
Combo weighting is the final step in construction: assigning frequencies to each combo so value, bluff, protection, and merge bands perform as intended. It is not enough to include twelve combos—you must weight them so the strongest play full-size, bluffs contribute fold equity, protection perseveres, and merge hands can flex without collapsing the entire range.

# Why it matters
Proper weighting controls density, calibrates pressure, and keeps your bluff/value ratios balanced against the pool. Blocker effects only matter when bluffs carry the right frequency, and EV protection arrives when you avoid over- or under-weighting any layer. Weighting keeps you from leaking by running too many bluffs or diluting value, especially near pressure thresholds.

# Rules of thumb
- Start with the value density, then work outward so the strongest combos anchor every street.
- Weight bluffs from the lowest-equity combos that still hold blockers to maintain fold equity.
- Protection weight focuses on fragile medium hands that shield the range without bloating.
- Merge weight should expand or contract based on whether the opponent is capped; it is the flexible layer.
- Avoid overweighting marginal bluffs, which invite calls before fold equity accrues.
- Avoid flattening value weight to maintain the leverage needed for pressure.
- Think of weights as the percentages that make each combo believable—not static counts.

# Mini example
Sixteen combos form the range: assign four full-weight value combos, four partial-weight bluffs that hold key blockers, four medium combos with protection weight to absorb aggression, and four merge combos that can flip to either intimidation or defense depending on the action. This allocation keeps volumes balanced while allowing shifts in pressure.

# Common mistakes
- Treating all bluffs as equal weight and overloading the bluff band.
- Ignoring blockers when choosing bluff weight, leaving fold equity on the table.
- Overweighting protection hands so the range looks passive.
- Over-thinning the merge band and losing the ability to switch roles.
- Misreading fragility zones and defending low-equity hands too hard.
- Forcing symmetrical weights in asymmetrical nodes where one side clearly controls leverage.

# Workflow
- Identify the value backbone and assign it full or near-full frequency.
- Select the bluff group and dial in their lower weights based on blockers.
- Insert protection weight for medium-strength combos that must defend.
- Check merge opportunities—hands that can pivot depending on the opponent.
- Verify density against pool tendencies to make sure you are not under- or over-weighted.
- Adjust thin value or thin bluff weights as needed to keep balance with adjacent bands.

# Mini-glossary
- weighting : the frequency assigned to a combo inside its strategic band.
- density : how many combos occupy a band and influence its strength.
- thin value : medium-high combos that still add EV with partial aggression.
- leverage weight : the pressure your weighted segment applies on opponents.
- protection weight : the portion of the range that stops enemy aggression.
- merge weight : the flexibility assigned to hands that can switch roles.
- blocker effect : how your cards remove opponent counter combos, impacting bluff weight.
- fragility : how easily a combo collapses if pressured too hard.
- pressure band : the portion of your range dedicated to applying aggression.
- adjustment loop : the iterative process of re-weighting after observing results.

# EV
Weighted ranges fine-tune aggression precision and reduce leaks in thin bands. When every combo has the right weight, bluff/value ratios stay healthy, protections defend without clogging the range, and the long-run EV reflects disciplined weighting rather than uncontrolled spikes.
