# What it is
Pressure mapping tracks where your ranges carry fold equity, where they lack leverage, and how pressure tiers unfold across the tree. It describes the nodes where aggression forces folds versus nodes where your initiative dies; unlike raw advantage, which measures hand quality, pressure mapping focuses on how density and blocker alignment convert equity into threats.

# Why it matters
Knowing the pressure landscape lets you spot fold-heavy layers, avoid over-pressuring robust ranges, and lock down EV by leaning only into zones where aggression works. When you incorrectly apply pressure in low-response areas, you waste equity; when you map pressure correctly, you strike while opponents hide behind capped or dense protections.

# Rules of thumb
- Pressure comes from top-end leverage combined with blocker alignment that removes common counter-combos.
- Low-pressure zones emerge where opponent density is high or the range is capped and already defending.
- Apply polar pressure versus wide, capped, fold-biased tiers that cannot absorb aggression.
- Reduce bluff weight when opponents present dense or uncapped ranges so you do not bleed value.
- Mid-pressure nodes rely on merge and protection lines instead of pure polarization.
- Pressure only matters if the fold equity exists in the node; otherwise keep the range honest.

# Mini example
Your range has a strong top-end density and layered blockers, while the opponent shows a capped structure with a thin bluff band. Apply high-pressure lines to mid-band combos and freeze their aggression; the responding range shrinks bluff weight, relies on protection/merge, and avoids leaving the mid-zone exploitable.

# Common mistakes
- Over-bluffing low-pressure nodes where fold equity is minimal.
- Misreading dense ranges as fold-biased and applying pressure that fails.
- Forcing polar actions where leverage is weak or capped.
- Ignoring mid-pressure merge/protection lines that actually carry EV.
- Attacking uncapped ranges without real fold equity behind the size.
- Overestimating blocker effects and leaning on them without backup.

# Workflow
- Assess density and top-end leverage to understand where pressure can operate.
- Find fold-biased tiers that cannot defend aggression peacefully.
- Classify zones into high, mid, or low pressure so your lines align accordingly.
- Assign value and bluff weights based on the selected pressure tier.
- Verify coverage so no gaps appear when opponents adjust.
- Adjust aggression upward or downward to match pool tendencies.

# Mini-glossary
- pressure mapping : mapping where your range can apply leverage versus where it cannot.
- leverage : the ability to force folds with aggression.
- top-end : the strongest segment of your range that anchors pressure.
- density zone : how crowded a part of the range is with combos.
- fold-biased tier : nodes where opponents tend to fold in response to pressure.
- mid-pressure line : spots requiring merge or protection weight instead of polarization.
- low-pressure zone : nodes where aggression earns little fold equity.
- merge pressure : the act of blending value and protection to keep pressure alive.
- blocker leverage : the dependence on blocking cards to raise bluff credibility.
- adjustment cycle : the repeat loop that recalibrates weights after each observation.

# EV
Pressure mapping stabilizes exploit aggression by aligning frequency with opponent leaks and reducing wasted bluffs. When you commit only in fold-heavy layers and back off in dense nodes, EV leaks shrink and your continued aggression becomes a predictable weapon rather than a gamble.
