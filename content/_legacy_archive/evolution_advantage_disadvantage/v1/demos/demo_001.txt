# Scenario
IP fires a small probe on a wet board, forcing OOP to judge who owns the structural advantage based on nut density and mid-band strength.

# Snapshot
Attacker: high top density with strong nut pairs, steady mid cohesion. Defender: lean mid-band, fewer high cards, but a few layered mix-ins for protection.

# Player task
Decide which side holds the range advantage and explain how top density and mid-band cohesion support that read.

# Expected reasoning
Attacker’s dense top-end keeps nut advantage, especially when mid cohesion lets them continue with confidence. Defender lacks matching top density and leans on a thinner mid layer, so they cannot respond with the same pressure. Cohesion favors the attacker because their bands connect, reducing the need to over-bluff. Defender’s protection mix is reactive, giving the attacker room to keep initiative.

# Correct line
Attacker has advantage: nut density plus cohesive mid band keeps initiatives.

# Why not the alternatives
- Defender advantage: ignores the top-denisty gap.
- Even: downgrades the cohesion that keeps the attacker aggressive.
- Defender initiative: misreads thin mid protections as control.
- Attacker lag: assumes the defender’s protection is proactive when it is reactive.
- Mid-band tie: overlooks how the attacker’s cohesion pressure keeps defender compressed.
