# What to do
Assess two abstract ranges and decide which player holds the structural advantage, citing top-end density and mid-band cohesion.

# Input
Range A: AA, KK, QQ, AK, AQ, KQ, QJ, JT, T9, 98, 87, 76. Range B: QQ, JJ, TT, 99, 88, AJs, KTs, QTs, J9s, T8s, 97s, 86s, 75s, 64s, 53s.

# Expected reasoning
Nut advantage comes from how many top combos anchor a range; A's big pairs plus AK and AQ give it the leverage. Density matters: B is packed with connectors and mid pairs, offering cohesion but lacking the highest combos. Mid-band cohesion supports advantage when the opponent lacks nut coverage; A’s mid-band is leaner yet still strong. Combined density vs top-end tells you advantage without needing actual board context.

# Correct answer
Range A has the advantage because its top-end density plus strong high cards dominates Range B’s more compressed mid-band, giving it leverage and pressure.

# Common mistakes
- Treating Range B’s abundant mid combos as a stronger advantage. 
- Confusing raw combo count with nut advantage.
- Ignoring A’s top-end pairs and assuming B’s density compensates. 
- Overvaluing connectors without blockers when deciding advantage. 
- Calling it equal because both ranges cover medium connectors.
