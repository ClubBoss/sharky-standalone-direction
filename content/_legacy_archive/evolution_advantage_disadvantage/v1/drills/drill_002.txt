# What to do
Decide which range carries the advantage when one has higher top pairs and the other shows denser mid-band weight, referencing nut advantage and density.

# Input
Range A: AA, KK, AK, AQ, AJs, KQs, QJs, JT, T9, 98. Range B: QQ, JJ, TT, 99, 88, 77, 66, 55, 44, 33, 22, KTs, QTs, J9s.

# Expected reasoning
Nut advantage hinges on top pairs and broadway combinations; Range A holds more of those. Density plays the mid-band: Range B is packed with mid pairs and connectors but lacks the high top-end. If mid-band dominates without matching nuts, the range may still lose initiative because the opponent capitalizes on top-end leverage. Assess how much pressure each structure can apply before concluding advantage.

# Correct answer
Range A has advantage; its superior top-end density and blocker coverage outmuscle Range B’s mid-band weight, giving it the structural edge.

# Common mistakes
- Treating Range B’s density as outright advantage despite missing top-end combos.
- Overvaluing Range A’s broadways and calling it polar while ignoring the density gap.
- Assuming equal leverage because both ranges have mid-layer presence.
- Ignoring the lack of blockers in Range B’s mid-band when deciding advantage.
- Flattening the view and saying no one has advantage.
