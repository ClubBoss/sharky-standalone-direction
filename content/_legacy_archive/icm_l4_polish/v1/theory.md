Overview: ICM L4 Polish sharpens decision-making when the cash-ICM stack structure leans into high ERF pressure. We treat this module as the final polishing lane before case studies, so every run of BTN/SB/BB interactions is tuned to payout leverage, bubble psychology, and the shrinking room for speculative play.

Core ICM Principles: SB versus BB dynamics begin with asymmetric stack risk. BB has invested one big blind and rarely wants to widen without solid connectivity, while SB must balance protection of a vulnerable stack with the option to steal. BTN can often barrel the weakest stack, but once ICM pressure concentrates on the bubble, BTN needs to keep enough fold equity to punish defenders who overfold. We watch how risk-to-reward ratios shift when the next elimination costs more chips than the blind structure, and we treat the short stack as an ICM-sensitive anchor while the larger stack shoulders the aggression.

Range Adjustments: Push/call/fold decisions need explicit bubble-aware bands. BTN shoves with hands that dominate SB/BB calling ranges and that carry blockers to their best bluffs; flatting without potential to turn the pot keeps ICM tax high. SB should tighten call ranges but keep the best suited connectors for spots where BB is likely to fold or overfold around the iconography of typical online bubble play. BB folds much of its bottom end but can defend with high-card combinations when SB is predictably wide. In all cases we keep the ICM bubble spread in mind: folding offsuit garbage saves more than a token chip, calling without fold equity burns the stack.

Practical Heuristics: Risk premium means we pay extra to avoid elimination. When you are the leader, lean on big stack pressure to force defenders into marginal flips; you can size up to rub more chips off them without fear. When you are short, push with hands that have decent showdown value and avoid grouping them with pure bluffs because the payout curve punishes a miss. Typical online tendencies favor smallish open raises and sticky callers, so our polish trains us to extend range when stack disparity favors our chips while shrinking it when the bubble premium rises. Payout leverage also matters: the jump from final table swaps or bonus credits changes what counts as a fold–call gap, so we rehearse walking that line in simulated high-stakes practice.

Common Leaks: Overcommitting to multiway pots with weak connectivity is the fastest way to bleed an ICM stack. Another leak is bluffing too often when you are ahead on the leaderboard; a shove that would earn fold equity vs a static stack instead becomes a solo coin flip that reduces your ladder height. Conversely, the short stack defending with tiny sneaky calls on obvious steals gives the leader extra fold equity and compresses payout spread. Finally, ignoring the ICM cost of a third-place finish—where your stack shrinks but you still miss the money jumps—makes you overvalue variance.

Tight versus loose play in these spots means choosing whether to lock up equity now or chase opponents into danger when every chip carries payout weight.
