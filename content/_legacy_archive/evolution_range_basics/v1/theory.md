# What it is
A poker range is the collection of hands you can have in a given situation; it is defined by how you weight every combination you might play, from the strongest holdings down to the bluffs you carry for leverage.

# Why it matters
Range discipline keeps opponents honest, protects vulnerable holdings, and ensures your betting looks consistent whether you are polarizing, merging, or defending. When your construction accounts for blockers, position, and equity realization, the river becomes a negotiation over EV edges instead of a guessing game.

# Rules of thumb
- Tighten your range when out of position or against aggressive stacking so weaker hands stop leaking equity.
- Loosen in position where you can price and maneuver opponents while keeping coverage aligned with the optimal construction.
- Linear ranges favor value-heavy, connected hands and keep your blocker coverage solid, while polar ranges mix top-value hands with heavy bluffs to deny equity; match the approach to stack depth and opponent tendencies.
- Adjust your range width based on opponent profile: the looser they are, the tighter you can be, and vice versa, but never abandon the concepts of coverage and balance.
- Plan equity realization by keeping a mix of hands that continue on safe boards and can bluff-catch or finish when equity spikes; prioritizing narrow equity prevents over-folding.
- Capping occurs when you stop adding strong hands or protectives as you reach manual limits; stop capping by adding the next relevant combos before locking in the rest of the range.
- Always reserve a portion of the range for protection (calls/checks) versus attacking lines so you do not allow straightforward exploitative pressure.

# Mini example
Facing a 3-bet from the button, you construct a tight defending range from the big blind: include the strongest suited aces for value, middle connectors for coverage, and a few medium pairs for protection. When you pick up blockers and position, you expand to combinations that can barrel on dynamic turns, letting your new range look polar without forgetting the linear base you need to realize equity.

# Common mistakes
- Bluffing too far down the ladder with bottom pair or worse; the resultant hands have poor equity when called.
- Merging without respect for blockers, turning what should have been a polar lineup into a transparent stack of middle-strength hands.
- Failing to protect calling ranges by ignoring hands that can defend on wet boards or against check-raises.
- Unintentionally capping because you stop adding premium hands after the first few combos.
- Ignoring the effect of position, treating out-of-position ranges like they belong to the button.

# Workflow
- Start with the base value range for the situation (open, defend, or 3-bet) and list the category of hands that should certainly be included.
- Layer in coverage considerations: add hands that block opponent bluffs, maintain nut coverage, and operate well on important textures.
- Decide how much equity is realized by each combo; keep ones that can continue or improve without folding under pressure.
- Reserve a fraction of the range for protection lines (calls/checks) so you can handle aggression without folding the bulk straight away.
- Snap the final weighting to a plan (e.g., frequency of bluffs, proportion of raises) that keeps the whole construction coherent.

# Mini-glossary
- linear : a tight, value-focused construction that keeps close to the strongest combos.
- polar : a spread range combining narrow value with wider bluffs that target opponent mistakes.
- capped : when a range stops gaining additional strong hands, leaving openings for exploitation.
- uncapped : a range that continues adding the best combos even as the rest of the mix grows.
- merge : blending polar and linear elements so the range can shift between value and protection.
- protection : the part of your range that defends against aggression with calls or checks instead of folding.
- blocker : a card in your hand that removes critical combinations from your opponent’s range.
- equity realization : the ability of a hand to cash out its theoretical equity through position and playability.
- coverage : ensuring that your range can respond to any board or opponent action without glaring holes.

# EV
Strong range construction reduces variance by keeping value investments protected and bluff EV positive. When you consistently weight each combination with its role—value, protection, or bluff—the long-run EV premium becomes the sum of solved frequencies rather than individual heroics.
